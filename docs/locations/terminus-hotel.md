## Chronology

- **1841.** Pyrmont Hotel built on the Harris Street and John Street corner. Owned by William Allison; tenant publican Charles Cameron, a Scots migrant brought to the colony by the Reverend John Dunmore Lang. Allison also built J.D. Lang's Presbyterian church in Mount Street. [S9]
- **1845.** Peter Brennan, born 1805 and apprenticed as a cooper to Joseph Clayton (a George Street cooper), takes the licence of the Coopers Arms. Brennan owns the Coopers Arms at 86 Harris Street and the adjacent house at 88 Harris Street, on the opposite side of Harris Street from the corner block. He holds the licence to 1860. [S9]
- **1846.** Charles Cameron departs. The Pyrmont Hotel is sold as a house and the licence lapses. [S9]
- **1853.** The corner site changes hands again, reopening as Land's End under publican James Pratt. [S9]
- **1858.** Coopers Arms first enters official records. [S9]
- **1860s.** Coopers Arms "crosses Harris Street", relocating to the corner block in place of Land's End. The original structure on the corner is a single-storey Harris Street building. [S9]
- **1898.** Sydney's Harris Street electric tram line opens, powered by the Ultimo power station. The corner of Harris and John Streets is the original Pyrmont terminus. [S9][S11]
- **c.1898 to c.1900.** Owners of the Coopers Arms add a two-storey wing and rename the pub the Terminus, capitalising on the new tram terminus immediately outside. The Pyrmont History Group dates this section as "Terminus Hotel, 1900". [S9]
- **1910.** A Balmain branch is added to the tram network, turning off Harris Street into Miller and Bank Streets and crossing the Glebe Island Bridge. The Harris and John Streets corner remains the established Pyrmont stop. [S9][S11]
- **1917.** Tooth & Co., NSW's largest brewer, demolishes the existing structure and rebuilds the present Federation-style building. The rebuild is driven by new licensing laws requiring accommodation and 10 am to 6 pm, six-days-a-week trading. The full-time Pyrmont tram service is also withdrawn this year. [S9][S11]
- **1950s.** The Ladies Parlour is merged with the Public Bar. [S9]
- **1953.** Last regular tram service through Pyrmont. [S9][S11]
- **1970s to early 1980s.** The McElwaine family runs the hotel. [S9]
- **1983.** The McElwaine family closes the pub. Pyrmont's industrial employers have folded, residents have moved out, and tighter drink-driving laws have ended the suburb's pub culture. The building and licence are bought by Susan and Isaac Wakil as a long-term investment. [S9]
- **1983 to 2016.** Building stands closed for 33 years, occupied intermittently by feral cats and squatters. [S9]
- **2016.** The Wakils begin selling their portfolio. The Terminus is acquired by new owners (David Mathlin and Binu Katari per commercial property reporting) for a reported 4.75 million dollars. [S3][S9]
- **2016 to 2018.** Restoration over approximately two years, reported cost upwards of 2 million dollars. [S3][S4]
- **2018.** Terminus Hotel reopens as a working pub after 35 years closed. [S3][S4]

## Architecture and site

- Corner pub site of 493.2 square metres at Harris and John Streets, Pyrmont. [S2]
- Named "Terminus" for its position at the original terminus of the Pyrmont electric tram line, opened 1898 from Railway Square along Harris Street and powered by the Ultimo power station. The Harris and John Streets corner was the established stop even after a Balmain branch was added in 1910. [S9][S11]
- Federation hotel form. The building visible today is the 1917 Tooth & Co. demolition-and-rebuild, not a remodel of the earlier Coopers Arms. The earlier Coopers Arms had been a single-storey Harris Street structure with a two-storey wing added around 1900 at the time of the rename. [S9]
- The site has held a licence almost continuously since 1841 under four names: Pyrmont Hotel, Land's End, Coopers Arms, Terminus. The Coopers Arms licence dates from 1845 but was originally on the opposite side of Harris Street, only crossing to the corner block in the 1860s. [S9]

**Wording to use.** "Corner pub" or "corner hotel" is accurate. "Federation hotel" is the heritage-listed descriptor for the 1917 building. The site has had multiple lives as a licensed premises: Pyrmont Hotel (1841), Land's End (1853), Coopers Arms (relocated to the corner in the 1860s, after a 1845 start across the street), and Terminus (renamed around 1900, rebuilt 1917). Avoid framing the building itself as 1863 or older. The current building is 1917 fabric on a site that has held a licence since 1841.

## Rooms and interiors

- Bar configuration before the 1950s: Public Bar (men only); Parlour (women if accompanied by a man); Ladies Parlour (women on their own). The Ladies Parlour was merged into the Public Bar in the 1950s. [S9]
- Tooth & Co.'s 1917 rebuild added accommodation rooms upstairs as part of compliance with the new licensing laws. [S9]
- Described in 2016 to 2018 reporting as a time capsule, with fit-out preserved from the closure period. [S3]

## Role in wider context

- The Terminus served the workforce of one of Sydney's most concentrated industrial peninsulas. Pyrmont's industries from the 1840s onward included shipbuilding (Australian Steam Navigation Company yards on Darling Island, with a patent slip handling 2,000-ton ships by 1855), sandstone quarrying ("yellowblock" was used across nineteenth-century Sydney; the quarries were nicknamed Paradise, Purgatory and Hellhole), the adjacent Glebe Island abattoirs (1860), Colonial Sugar Refining Co. on the northern point, the Pyrmont power station, and the wharves that handled the colony's wheat, timber and coal trade. [S10]
- The 1917 Tooth & Co. rebuild, the Ladies-Parlour-into-Public-Bar merger of the 1950s, and the 1983 closure track the broader arc of NSW pub regulation and the post-war collapse of inner-Sydney industry. [S9][S10]
- Pyrmont History Group puts the suburb's pub culture in the same frame: when the abattoirs and rail yards emptied out in the 1970s and 1980s, "the area was full of derelict sites, abandoned workplaces" (per Fitzgerald, Dictionary of Sydney). The Terminus was one of many casualties. [S10]
- Pyrmont redeveloped from the 1990s onward (Pyrmont Point Park, 1995; Star City Casino on the powerhouse site; high-density residential towers). The 2018 Terminus reopening sits inside that longer gentrification arc. [S10]
- Tooth & Co.'s tied-house network connected the Terminus to the wider NSW brewing trade. The Tooth & Co. corporate archive is held at the Noel Butlin Archives Centre, Australian National University (R3 lead). [S9]

## People

- **William Allison.** Owner of the original 1841 Pyrmont Hotel building on the corner. Also built the Reverend John Dunmore Lang's Presbyterian church in Mount Street. [S9]
- **Charles Cameron.** First publican on the corner. Scots migrant brought to the colony by Lang. Tenanted the Pyrmont Hotel from 1841; departed 1846. [S9]
- **James Pratt.** Manager of Land's End on the corner from 1853, after the Pyrmont Hotel closed. [S9]
- **Joseph Clayton.** George Street cooper. Employed Peter Brennan per the 1828 Census. Owned earlier Coopers Arms hotels in George Street (1830) and Sussex Street (1833). Died 1836. [S9]
- **Elizabeth Clayton (later Brennan).** Joseph Clayton's widow. Held the licences of the two city Coopers Arms hotels in 1836. Married Peter Brennan in 1837. [S9]
- **Peter Brennan.** Born 1805. Apprenticed cooper. Held the Pyrmont Coopers Arms licence 1845 to 1860; owned 86 and 88 Harris Street. The relocation of the Coopers Arms to the corner block happens after his tenure. [S9]
- **The McElwaine family.** Last active proprietors of the Terminus. Closed the pub in 1983. (Also spelled MacElwaine in some Pyrmont History Group references.) [S9]
- **Susan and Isaac Wakil.** Sydney property investors. Bought the building and licence in 1983 as a long-term investment. Held it for 33 years; began divesting their portfolio around 2016, at which point the Terminus was sold to the current owners. [S9]
- **David Mathlin and Binu Katari.** Owners from 2016. Led the restoration and the 2018 reopening. [S3]
- **Shirley Fitzgerald.** City Historian for the City of Sydney 1987 to 2009. Author of the Dictionary of Sydney's Pyrmont entry [S10] and of *Terminus: The Pub That Sydney Forgot* (Simon & Schuster Australia, 2018). [S6] The book remains the most likely R3 primary-source consolidation for this site.
- **Reverend John Dunmore Lang.** Presbyterian minister and migration sponsor. Brought Charles Cameron and other Scots migrants to the colony; commissioned the Mount Street church from Allison. [S9]

## Redevelopment and current use

- Building and licence held by Susan and Isaac Wakil from 1983 to 2016 as part of their long-term Sydney property portfolio. The Wakils were known for holding heritage buildings deliberately empty for decades; the Terminus fits that pattern. [S9]
- Building stood closed for 33 years, occupied intermittently by feral cats and squatters. [S9]
- Wakils began divesting their portfolio around 2016. The Terminus was acquired by David Mathlin and Binu Katari for a reported 4.75 million dollars. [S3][S9]
- Restoration over approximately two years at a reported cost upwards of 2 million dollars. [S3]
- Reopened as a pub, bar and hotel in 2018 after 35 years closed. [S3][S4]
- Now trades as a dog-friendly inner-Sydney pub with a beer garden. [S7]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back to at least one verified fact.

1. **Four pubs on one corner, 180 years of continuous licence.** The site has held a licence almost continuously since 1841 under four names: Pyrmont Hotel (1841, William Allison), Land's End (1853, James Pratt), Coopers Arms (relocated to the corner in the 1860s, having opened across Harris Street in 1845), and Terminus (renamed around 1900). The 1917 Tooth & Co. rebuild stands on an 1841 licence. [S9]
2. **Named for a real terminus.** Renamed around 1900 to capitalise on the electric tram that started running up Harris Street in 1898, powered by the Ultimo power station and terminating at the pub's front door. The Harris and John Streets corner remained the established Pyrmont stop even after a Balmain branch was added in 1910. Full-time tram service was withdrawn in 1917, the same year Tooth & Co. rebuilt the pub. [S9][S11]
3. **Rebuilt by a brewer, driven by a law change.** The present Federation-style building is the 1917 Tooth & Co. demolition and rebuild, not a remodel of the earlier Coopers Arms. The rebuild responded to NSW licensing reform that cut trading hours to 10 am to 6 pm, six days a week, and required hotels to provide accommodation. Tooth & Co. was NSW's largest brewer and ran a tied-house network. [S9]
4. **Thirty-three years with the lights off.** The McElwaine family closed the pub in 1983 as Pyrmont's industrial base collapsed, residents left, and tighter drink-driving laws ended the suburb's pub culture. Susan and Isaac Wakil bought the building as a long-term investment and held it closed for 33 years. [S9]
5. **Industrial Pyrmont's last pub standing.** The Terminus served a suburb whose sandstone quarries were nicknamed Paradise, Purgatory and Hellhole, and whose workforce had to move the Glebe Island abattoirs, the CSR sugar refinery, Darling Island shipyards, and the Pyrmont power station. The Terminus survived the 1970s and 1980s collapse that demolished or emptied most of Pyrmont's industrial infrastructure. [S10]
6. **A complete Lost Collective arc.** Photographed while still dormant, then reopened. The Terminus has moved through the full ruin-to-restoration cycle that Lost Collective otherwise only partially documents.
7. **The historian who wrote the book.** Shirley Fitzgerald, City Historian for the City of Sydney 1987 to 2009, wrote both the Dictionary of Sydney Pyrmont entry and a 2018 book-length history of the Terminus. The pub has an unusually well-documented backstory for a corner hotel. [S6][S10]

**Angles to avoid.** "Abandoned" in the present tense (the Terminus has been operating since 2018). The "1863 Coopers Arms Inn" framing from R1 commercial property coverage. The Coopers Arms only reached the corner block in the 1860s after starting at 86 Harris Street in 1845, and the building visible today is 1917 fabric regardless. Do not describe the 1917 intervention as a "remodel"; it was a demolition and rebuild.

## Things worth noting in photographs

- Corner frontage at Harris and John Streets. The current building is 1917 Federation-era Tooth & Co. fabric on a site that has held a licence since 1841. [S9]
- Federation-era exterior detail, cited as a rare level of intact fabric for a hotel of this period. [S2]
- Closed-period interior as photographed between 1983 and 2018: a fit-out that walked the building through 33 years of vacancy, overlaid on the 1950s reconfiguration (Ladies Parlour merged into the Public Bar) and the original 1917 Tooth & Co. bar plan. [S9]
- Upstairs accommodation rooms added by the 1917 rebuild to comply with NSW licensing reform. [S9]
- The Harris Street frontage once had electric tram tracks terminating outside the front door; the tram poles and overhead wiring were a feature of the streetscape from 1898 to 1953. [S9][S11]
