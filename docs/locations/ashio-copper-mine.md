## Chronology

- **Pre-Meiji era.** Mine operated for centuries prior to Meiji modernisation. [S1]
- **1877.** Mine becomes the property of Furukawa Ichibei, backed financially by Shibusawa Eiichi. Modernisation and expansion follows, using foreign mining engineering expertise. [S1][S2]
- **Mid-1880s.** Residents on the Watarase and Tone river watersheds begin to observe pollution effects: dying fish, poor harvests, ill health. [S1]
- **1890.** Shōzō Tanaka elected to the National Diet. Major flood on the Watarase River spreads mine pollution widely. [S1][S3]
- **1891.** Tanaka delivers a speech in the Diet calling on the government to close the mine because of pollution. [S1][S3]
- **1896.** Pollution at its peak. River network spreads pollutants (arsenic, cadmium, lead, mercury among others) across around 40,000 hectares downstream, covering five prefectures and part of Tokyo. [S1]
- **1897.** Third Mine Pollution Prevention Order issued by the government. Farmers march on Tokyo twice; the second march numbers around 4,000 participants. [S1]
- **1907.** Ashio miners' riots. [S1]
- **1973.** Mine's Excavation Department closed. [S1]
- **1980.** "Ashio Copper Mine Sightseeing" tourist attraction and Furukawa Ashio History Museum open. [S1]
- **2008.** Mine designated a National Historic Site of Japan. [S1]

## Architecture and site

- Mine is located in a mountainous area of the northern Kantō region. [S1]
- Visitors can now ride a trolley train down the Tsūdō mine shaft, a national historic site. [S1][S2]
- Surface infrastructure includes historical mining buildings and the associated Furukawa Ashio History Museum. [S1][S2]

**Wording to use.** Use "Ashio Copper Mine" on first reference and "Ashio Dōzan" (足尾銅山) parenthetically where appropriate. The site is in Ashio, now administratively part of Nikkō City, Tochigi Prefecture. "Incident" (事件) or "Affair" is the conventional shorthand for the pollution scandal.

## Role in wider context

- **Japan's first major industrial pollution disaster.** The Ashio Copper Mine Incident is widely regarded as the start of Japan's environmental movement. [S1][S3][S4]
- **Key player in Japan's modernisation.** Ashio was one of the major copper producers underpinning Meiji industrialisation. [S2]
- **Early labour history.** Site of the 1907 miners' riots, one of Japan's notable early industrial labour actions. [S1]

---

## People

- **Furukawa Ichibei (1832 to 1903).** Industrialist. Acquired the mine in 1877 and drove its modernisation with backing from Shibusawa Eiichi. [S1][S2]
- **Shōzō Tanaka / 田中正造 (1841 to 1913).** Politician and activist. Elected to the National Diet in 1890. Led the political opposition to the mine's pollution. Set up a mining-damage office at Unryu Temple in Watarase Village, Gunma Prefecture. Widely considered the father of Japan's environmental movement. [S1][S3]
- **Shibusawa Eiichi.** Provided financial backing for Furukawa's modernisation of the mine from 1877. [S1]
