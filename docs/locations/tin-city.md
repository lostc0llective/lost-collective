## Critical framing correction (2026-04-26)

**Tin City is in the Stockton Bight sand dunes, Port Stephens LGA, NSW.
It is not on Lake Macquarie.** Confirmed Tier 1 via NSW National Parks
and Wildlife Service. Lake Macquarie is a separate LGA roughly 30 km
south of the Stockton Bight. Any Lost Collective store, blog, or
metadata copy that places Tin City on Lake Macquarie is wrong and
should be corrected. [S7][S8]

## Chronology

- **Many thousands of years before present to today.** The Worimi
  people are the traditional owners of the Port Stephens region.
  Aboriginal occupation of the Bight is recorded in middens, fish
  traps, and scarred trees. Middens are dominated by pipi (Donax /
  Plebidonax deltoides) with mud whelk and cockle. 186 Aboriginal
  heritage sites are recorded on the Bight (Dean-Jones 1990 recorded
  approximately 70; the Newcastle Bight Committee a further 116).
  Specific midden ages cited in the public-facing Worimi record run
  over 1,200 years; broader Worimi occupation of the region is
  described as extending back many thousands of years. [S9][S10][S15]
- **Late 19th century.** Two tin sheds erected on the dunes (in what
  is now the Bobs Farm / Salt Ash area) to hold provisions for
  sailors shipwrecked on Stockton Beach. The trigger was not a single
  wreck but the cumulative wreck cluster: Cawarra (1866), Berbice
  (1888), Colonist (1894), Wendouree (1898), Lindus (1899), Adolphe
  (1904), among the 59 ships eventually lost on Newcastle shores.
  [S1][S14]
- **1930s.** During the Great Depression, a group of squatters
  construct a series of tin shacks at the site. Settlement peaks
  around 36 to 38 structures (original 2 provision sheds plus around
  36 squatter additions). [S1][S4]
- **World War II.** The shacks are torn down to make way for an Army
  camp. When the squatters return after the war, their huts have
  been buried by migrating sand. They rebuild using driftwood and
  discarded military crates that washed up on the beach. [S1]
- **Post-war.** The settlement stabilises at a small number of
  shacks. [S1]
- **1990s.** Worimi Local Aboriginal Land Council lodges land claims
  over Crown land at Stockton Bight under the NSW Aboriginal Land
  Rights Act 1983. [S9]
- **2001.** In-principle agreement between Worimi LALC and the NSW
  Government on the future of the Bight. [S9]
- **2005 to 2006.** Lease Agreement negotiated between Worimi LALC
  and the NSW Government. Signed by Environment Minister Bob Debus.
  [S9]
- **1 February 2007.** Land granted to Worimi LALC and simultaneously
  leased back to the NSW Government. Lands gazetted under Part 4A of
  the NPW Act 1974 as three reserves: Worimi National Park, Worimi
  State Conservation Area, and Worimi Regional Park. Approximately
  4,200 to 4,436 ha total. [S1][S7][S9]
- **2010 to 2014.** Public consultation on the Worimi Conservation
  Lands Plan of Management. [S11]
- **2015.** Plan of Management adopted. Establishes the licence
  system that governs shack occupation. "No new shacks, no
  rebuilding after destruction" comes from this instrument. [S11][S12]
- **Modern era.** 11 shacks remain. The settlement is administered by
  the Worimi Conservation Lands Board of Management (13 members:
  8 registered Worimi Aboriginal Owners, plus Worimi LALC, councils,
  conservation groups, neighbours, and NPWS representatives), under
  the "Protect, Connect, Respect" framing. [S9][S11]

## Architecture and site

- Built from corrugated iron ("tin"), driftwood, and repurposed
  military crates. [S1][S4]
- 11 single-storey shacks, scattered across a section of the
  Stockton Bight dune field. [S1][S4]
- Setting: open sand dunes. The Stockton Bight contains "the largest
  mobile coastal sand mass in the Southern Hemisphere" (NPWS
  wording). The dune field stretches approximately 32 km from
  Stockton (Newcastle) to Anna Bay. Dunes reach over 30 m with slopes
  up to 60°. The dune field migrates approximately 4 m per year
  northward. [S7][S8]
- Vulnerable to burial by migrating sand, wind erosion, and storm
  surge. [S1]

**Wording to use.**

- "Tin City" is the collective name for the group of shacks.
- "Stockton Bight" or "Stockton Bight sand dunes" for the setting.
- "Worimi Conservation Lands" for the surrounding protected area.
- "Worimi country" or "Worimi people" for the traditional owners.
- "The largest mobile coastal sand mass in the Southern Hemisphere"
  is the NPWS wording for the dune field. Avoid "largest moving sand
  dune" (singular) — the precise term is "sand mass."
- Do not write "Lake Macquarie." Tin City is not on Lake Macquarie.

## Shacks and materials

- 11 shacks today. [S1][S4]
- Materials: corrugated iron, driftwood, repurposed military crates.
  [S1]
- Construction is informal and incremental, built up over decades of
  repair and replacement. [S1]
- Original 19th century tin sheds were for shipwreck provisions, not
  habitation. The habitation layer is a 20th century Depression-era
  overlay. [S1]
- Peak count of approximately 36 to 38 structures occurred in the
  1930s Depression. Reduced to a small post-war number by Army
  demolition + sand burial during WWII. [S1][S4]

## Heritage status

- Tin City itself is not individually heritage listed at R2.
- The surrounding **Worimi Conservation Lands** are a protected
  reserve jointly managed by Worimi people and NSW NPWS, gazetted
  under Part 4A of the National Parks and Wildlife Act 1974.
  [S7][S9]
- 186 Aboriginal heritage sites are recorded on the Stockton Bight
  (Dean-Jones 1990: ~70 sites; Newcastle Bight Committee: 116 more).
  Predominantly middens, fish traps, and scarred trees. [S10]
- Middens are dominated by pipi (Donax / Plebidonax deltoides) with
  mud whelk and cockle. [S10]

## Role in wider context

- Tin City sits within a landscape of multiple layers of occupation:
  Worimi presence extending back many thousands of years; 19th
  century European shipwreck and salvage economy; Great Depression
  squatting; World War II coastal defence; post-war re-establishment;
  21st century joint management of the Conservation Lands. [S1][S9]
- Often described in journalism as the last legal squatter settlement
  in Australia (Newcastle Herald, Australian Geographic, Port
  Stephens Examiner). The phrase is journalism-grade rather than
  formal NPWS or scholarly framing; attribute when using. [S4][S5]
- Used as a filming location for the original Mad Max (1979).
  Verifiable across journalism and academic sources; specific shacks
  on screen are not confirmed in available production records.
  [S4][S6]

## People

### Named shack-holders (from Newcastle Herald reporting)

- **Alwyn Garland** (66 at time of 2013 reporting). Former iron
  worker. No biological children of his own; raised his long-time
  partner's five sons at the shack. [S4]
- **Darren Stuart.** Pictured shack owner. [S4]

The Worimi Conservation Lands Board of Management administers the
licence system that governs shack occupation. Shacks cannot be sold;
they pass to family or friends. [S11]

### Worimi co-management

- **Worimi Local Aboriginal Land Council.** Holds freehold title to
  the Worimi Conservation Lands. Leases the land back to the NSW
  Government. Sits on the Board of Management. [S13]
- **Bob Debus.** As NSW Environment Minister, signed the Lease
  Agreement that led to the 1 February 2007 land grant. [S9]
- **Worimi Conservation Lands Board of Management.** 13 members:
  8 registered Worimi Aboriginal Owners; the remainder Worimi LALC,
  councils, conservation groups, neighbours, and NPWS. Adopted the
  "Protect, Connect, Respect" framing for the first Plan of
  Management. [S9]

## Redevelopment and current use

- No redevelopment. The settlement is frozen at its current size
  under the WCL Plan of Management 2015 licence system. No new
  shacks; no rebuilding after destruction. [S11]
- Tin City is accessible only via NPWS-licensed 4WD operators or on
  foot across the dunes. [S7][S8]
- Day visits are popular; overnight access is restricted. [S8]
- Worimi-led guided dune safaris to Tin City are run under NPWS
  arrangements. [S8]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties
back to at least one verified fact.

1. **Eleven shacks left.** A specific number, in a specific place
   (Stockton Bight, not Lake Macquarie). [S1][S4]
2. **Often called the last legal squatter settlement in Australia.**
   Attribute the phrase to Newcastle Herald or Australian
   Geographic; do not assert it as a formal NPWS or scholarly
   designation. [S4][S5]
3. **Depression era, war era, post-war rebuild.** The shacks carry
   the layers of the 20th century in their fabric. [S1]
4. **Buried and rebuilt.** When the army evicted the squatters
   during WWII, the sand buried their huts. They rebuilt with
   driftwood and military crates that washed up after the war. [S1]
5. **Many thousands of years of Worimi presence.** The shacks are a
   layer over a much older landscape. 186 Aboriginal heritage sites
   on the Bight; middens dominated by pipi. [S10][S15]
6. **No new building.** A settlement that can only shrink. The
   licence system in the WCL Plan of Management 2015 makes it
   formal. [S11]
7. **Migrating dunes.** The largest mobile coastal sand mass in the
   Southern Hemisphere (NPWS). Approximately 4 m of northward
   movement per year. Shacks can be swallowed. [S7]
8. **Returned land.** The Bight was returned to Worimi ownership in
   2007 and is now jointly managed. Tin City sits on Worimi country
   under a licence administered with the Worimi Conservation Lands
   Board of Management. [S9]
9. **Mad Max landscape.** Filming location for the 1979 original.
   Use carefully — the dunes feature, but specific shacks on screen
   are not confirmed. [S4][S6]

**Angles to avoid.**

- **Do not place Tin City on Lake Macquarie.** It is at Stockton
  Bight, Port Stephens. [S7]
- Do not describe Tin City as abandoned: the shacks are occupied and
  privately held under licence. [S11]
- Do not describe Stockton Bight as "uninhabited wilderness": Worimi
  people have occupied the country for many thousands of years.
  [S9][S15]
- Do not assert "12,000 years" of midden age. The publicly cited
  ages run over 1,200 years for specific middens; broader regional
  Worimi occupation is "many thousands" or "tens of thousands." Drop
  the specific 12,000 figure unless an archaeological date is
  available. [S10][S15]
- Do not assert a specific 1920 squatter's lease. The "100-year
  lease signed in 1920" is widely repeated in journalism but no
  Tier 1 deed has been found. [S4]
- Do not lean on Mad Max imagery without attribution.

## Claims to verify (do not use in copy yet)

- **"Last legal squatter settlement in Australia."** Widely repeated
  in journalism [S4][S5] but not used by NPWS or in scholarly
  housing histories surfaced at R2. Either attribute the phrase or
  soften.
- **Mad Max specific shacks on screen.** Filming location consensus
  is solid at Tier 2 [S4][S6] but no Tier 1 production-record
  citation was located. Do not assert which shacks appeared in
  frame.
- **"1920 100-year squatter's lease."** Widely cited in journalism
  and travel blogs. No Tier 1 archival deed surfaced at R2. Treat as
  folklore until R3 surfaces an instrument.
- **Specific shipwreck that prompted the original tin sheds.** The
  origin is the cumulative wreck cluster, not a single ship. The
  most authoritative single-wreck inquiry would be the Berbice (1888)
  via Hunter Living Histories [S14], but the sheds-as-response
  framing is broader.
- **Exact midden ages.** Public Worimi record cites "over 1,200
  years" for some middens; broader regional occupation is described
  as "many thousands of years" or "tens of thousands of years." Tier
  1 archaeological dating reports for specific Stockton Bight middens
  not yet sourced. R3.
- **Total recorded shack count over time.** Peak around 36 to 38 in
  the 1930s; 11 today. Decade-by-decade trajectory not yet captured.
- **IMDb production record for Mad Max (1979) location list.** Not
  retrievable in this round; worth a future direct check.
