## Chronology

- **22 May 1913.** NSW Cement Lime and Coal Company registered. [S9]
- **August 1913.** Company floated with £200,000 capital under chairman
  James Angus. Company purchases 100 acres from local farmer John
  Lloyd Junior for £2,000 to establish the plant and the town. [S1][S9]
- **1913 to 1914.** Krupp Bremen contracted to supply plant machinery.
  At WWI outbreak the machinery is interned in Portuguese West Africa.
  Managing director Frank Oakden travels to the USA and England to
  source replacement plant. [S1]
- **14 August 1915.** First land sale at the new town. 200 lots
  released. Town surveyed by local surveyor James Dawson. The town is
  originally proclaimed as "Candos" (an acronym of the company
  directors' surnames). [S1][S15]
- **1915.** Postmaster General requires the name to change to avoid
  confusion with Chandos, SA. Town becomes "Kandos." [S15]
- **August 1916.** Cement production begins. [S1]
- **11 January 1918.** Kandos proclaimed an urban area. [S1]
- **1919.** Methodist Church (Spanish Mission style) built, designed
  by works manager Floyd S. Richards. The building is now the Kandos
  Museum and is on the Australian Heritage Database. [S2]
- **1920.** Kandos becomes the first place in Australia with concrete
  electricity poles. [S1]
- **1928 to 1932.** Kandos cement supplied to the Sydney Harbour
  Bridge as the sole cement supplier under contract to Dorman Long &
  Co. Confirmed in Lawrence Ennis's 1932 souvenir booklet *Bond of
  Empire: Story of Construction of Bridge*, page 48: "cement - Kandos
  Cement Company Ltd." Tasmanian competitor Goliath/Railton publicly
  excluded by Ennis in July 1929. [S6]
- **1929.** Australian Portland Cement Co (Victoria) acquires the
  operating assets of Australian Cement Ltd Fyansford and Kandos
  Cement Co Ltd / Kandos Collieries Ltd. [S9]
- **1920s to 1930s.** Kandos cement also supplied to Sydney City
  Circle railway works, Glebe Island Wheat Silos, Mark Foy's
  additions, Royal Automobile Club, and Lane Cove Road works
  (cross-verification in R3 recommended). [S3]
- **1973.** Pioneer Concrete and CSR Limited jointly acquire
  Australian and Kandos Cement (Holdings) Ltd. [S9]
- **2003.** Cement Australia formed as a Holcim Australia and
  Heidelberg Materials / Hanson Australia joint venture. Becomes the
  final operator of the Kandos plant. [S9]
- **2007.** Cement Australia invests $10 million in plant
  modernisation, including the installation of a Kiln 6 stack. [S7]
- **11 July 2011.** Cement Australia CEO Chris Leon tells Kandos
  staff of closure in a Thursday morning meeting. [S7]
- **September 2011.** Plant closes. 98 redundancies over four months.
  34 offered roles at Cement Australia's Gladstone, Bulwer Island
  (QLD) or Railton (Tas) plants. Cited reasons: "dated, inefficient
  technology and high fixed costs"; "considerable distance from the
  market." [S7] Sibelco salvages the mid-section of Kiln 5 for use at
  the Charbon lime plant (subsequently also closed). [S11]
- **18 December 2013.** Mid-Western Regional Council approves a
  demolition DA from Cement Australia (asbestos removal, dam
  decontamination, possible explosives use, on-site concrete
  crushing). [S8]
- **2010s onwards.** Kandos Museum becomes the principal custodian of
  plant history. Campaigns to retain Kiln 5 and develop a "national
  Museum of Concrete." [S3][S16]
- **2013 onwards.** Cementa biennial contemporary art festival
  established as a cultural reinvention of Kandos, founded by writer
  and curator Ann Finegan. Editions documented include Cementa19,
  Cementa22, Cementa24. [S13][S14]
- **20 June 2025.** Site sold by Cabmon Property to Cenagen. [S1][S11]
- **October 2025 onwards.** Cenagen proposes a $1 billion waste-to-
  fuel plant: 1,500 tonnes/day gasification facility producing
  approximately 91,000 tonnes/year of green methanol from mixed
  municipal and business waste, plus a 10-hectare greenhouse using
  purified CO2. If approved as a State Significant Development:
  300 to 350 permanent jobs. Proposal contested in local press over
  NSW policy compliance. [S11][S12]

## Architecture and site

- Industrial complex developed from 1913 on the original 100-acre
  holding. Site has expanded to approximately 50 hectares across the
  operational life. [S1][S11]
- Key plant elements established by 1916: imported kilns (sourced
  from USA and UK after Krupp internment), stacks, silos, powerhouse,
  aerial ropeway from the limestone quarry, rail sidings, and a
  dedicated railway station. [S1]
- Adjacent limestone quarry fed the plant via aerial ropeway. [S1]
- Kandos itself is a purpose-built cement-works town, surveyed by
  James Dawson in 1915. Streets named for company officials: Angus
  Avenue (chairman), Buchanan, White, Rodgers, Jaques, McDonald,
  Noyes, Davies. [S1]
- Predates other purpose-built Australian industrial towns including
  Yallourn (SECV, 1921), Wittenoom (CSR/Hancock, 1947), and Mary
  Kathleen (Rio Tinto, 1956). [S1]

**Wording to use.** "Kandos Cement Works" or "Kandos cement plant"
(industry register). The founding company was the "NSW Cement Lime
and Coal Company." The operating subsidiary was "Kandos Cement Co
Ltd." When referring to cement supplied to Sydney landmarks, use
"Kandos cement" rather than any generic "local cement" phrasing. The
town spelling was "Candos" only briefly in 1915; do not use this in
copy referring to any later period.

## Plant and operations

- **Raw material.** Limestone from the adjacent quarry, transported
  to the works by aerial ropeway. [S1]
- **Product.** Portland cement, produced via rotary kilns under the
  dry process method. [S2][S11]
- **Kilns.** Multiple kilns installed across the operational life.
  Confirmed at R2: Kiln 5 and Kiln 6.
  - **Kiln 5.** The kiln specifically associated with Sydney Harbour
    Bridge cement production (1928 to 1932). The mid-section was
    salvaged by Sibelco in 2011 for the Charbon lime plant
    (subsequently closed). The remainder is the subject of an ongoing
    Kandos Museum preservation campaign. [S3][S11]
  - **Kiln 6 stack.** Installed during the 2007 $10 million plant
    upgrade. Designed and installed by an external engineer
    self-identified in industry-source comments. [S7][S11]
  - Kilns 1, 2, 3, and 4 not yet documented in available sources.
    Bruce A. Fleming's two-volume *History of Kandos Cement and
    Collieries* (Kandos Museum) is the print source most likely to
    resolve the full kiln record. R3 / offline.
- **Ancillary plant.** Stacks, silos, powerhouse, railway sidings,
  and a dedicated railway station, all established 1913 to 1916. [S1]
- **Closure condition.** By 2011 Cement Australia considered the
  factory outdated by modern cement-production standards, with high
  fixed costs and considerable distance from market. The 2007 $10M
  upgrade did not save it. [S7]

## Heritage status

- Bankstown of Kandos: Kandos Cement Works is **NOT listed on the NSW
  State Heritage Register** as of 2026-04-26. HMS searches return no
  results for "Kandos cement." [S10]
- **No Engineering Heritage Australia marker** for the cement works
  (no EHIM, EHNM, or EHM listing). [S10]
- **Mid-Western Regional Local Environmental Plan 2012** includes
  general objectives "to protect the settings of Mudgee, Gulgong,
  Kandos and Rylstone" but no specific Schedule 5 heritage entry for
  the cement works was located in available search results. The
  council's December 2013 demolition DA approval is consistent with
  no statutory listing at the works scale. [S17][S8]
- The **Kandos Museum** (former Methodist Church, Spanish Mission
  style, 1919, designed by Floyd S. Richards) is on the Australian
  Heritage Database. This is the museum building, not the cement
  works. [S2]
- Community-led preservation efforts have targeted Kiln 5
  specifically and a wider proposed national Museum of Concrete.
  [S3][S16]

## Role in wider context

- **Purpose-built industrial town.** The cement plant preceded and
  created the settlement of Kandos. [S1]
- **Cement supplied to the Sydney Harbour Bridge (verified Tier 1
  via Ennis 1932).** Kandos was the sole cement supplier to the
  bridge under contract to Dorman Long & Co. The Tasmanian Goliath
  plant at Railton, although it held a 1924 contract, was excluded;
  Lawrence Ennis stated publicly in July 1929 that "Railton cement
  is not used in the Sydney Harbour Bridge." Ennis's 1932 souvenir
  booklet *Bond of Empire*, page 48, lists the suppliers and names
  Kandos Cement Company Ltd. [S6]
- **Cement supplied to Sydney City Circle railway and other
  Sydney landmarks.** Including Glebe Island Wheat Silos, Royal
  Automobile Club, Mark Foy's additions, and Lane Cove Road works.
  These are sourced from the Mudgee Guardian / Kandos Museum tradition
  [S3] and have not been individually cross-verified to contemporary
  contract records; flagged R3.
- **Sydney Opera House cement supply: NOT verified.** See "Claims to
  verify" below. The most authoritative engineering source on the
  Opera House (Engineering Heritage Australia) does not name a cement
  supplier, and the Kandos History defence of the Harbour Bridge
  claim does not extend the same primary-source defence to the Opera
  House. Pull from copy until R3 confirms.
- **Closure ended 97 years of continuous cement production** and
  removed the town's principal employer. [S1][S2][S7]

## Redevelopment and current use

- Closed September 2011. [S7]
- Demolition approved December 2013. Significant parts of the plant
  have been or are being dismantled. [S8]
- Kiln 5 mid-section salvaged 2011 by Sibelco for the Charbon lime
  plant; remainder is subject of preservation campaign. [S3][S11]
- Kandos Museum is the principal custodian of the site's history and
  has campaigned to save Kiln 5 and to establish a national Museum of
  Concrete. [S3][S16]
- Site sold by Cabmon Property to Cenagen on 20 June 2025. [S1][S11]
- Cenagen proposal (2025): $1 billion waste-to-fuel plant on the
  50-hectare site. 1,500 tonnes/day gasification, ~91,000 tonnes/year
  green methanol from mixed municipal and business waste, plus a
  10-hectare greenhouse using purified CO2. If approved as State
  Significant Development: 300 to 350 permanent jobs. Faces NSW
  policy challenges; contested in local press. [S11][S12]
- Cementa biennial contemporary art festival continues as a cultural
  layer over post-industrial Kandos. [S13][S14]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back
to at least one verified fact.

1. **The town the cement works built.** Kandos exists because the
   plant was built. The town was originally Candos, an acronym of
   the company directors' surnames, until the Postmaster General
   ruled the name change in 1915. [S1][S15]
2. **Cement that built the Harbour Bridge.** Verified via Ennis's
   1932 *Bond of Empire*: Kandos was the sole cement supplier to the
   Sydney Harbour Bridge between 1928 and 1932. [S6] Strongest
   industrial-heritage hook for the location.
3. **Krupp interned, plant rebuilt.** Original German plant machinery
   was interned in Portuguese West Africa at WWI outbreak. MD Frank
   Oakden replaced it from the USA and England. The plant that built
   the Harbour Bridge was the second one Kandos installed. [S1]
4. **97 years of production.** 1916 to 2011. One product, one town,
   almost a century. [S1][S7]
5. **Obsolete by modern standards.** Closed because modern cement
   production had moved on, not because demand had disappeared. A
   $10M 2007 upgrade did not save it. [S7]
6. **Kiln 5.** The specific piece of plant that made Harbour Bridge
   cement. Half-salvaged in 2011 by Sibelco; the rest is the subject
   of an ongoing Kandos Museum preservation campaign. [S3][S11]
7. **First in Australia for concrete electricity poles, 1920.** A
   small, citable detail that anchors Kandos's role at the leading
   edge of Australian concrete technology. [S1]
8. **From cement to art to fuel.** Closure (2011), Cementa Festival
   (2013 onwards), Cenagen waste-to-methanol proposal (2025). The
   site has had three distinct phases of post-industrial reinvention.
   [S13][S11]

**Angles to avoid.**

- Anything that implies Kandos was the sole source of all Sydney's
  cement. It was the sole supplier to the Sydney Harbour Bridge
  (verified) and a major supplier to other Sydney landmarks
  (claimed; not all individually verified). "Supplied the cement for
  the Sydney Harbour Bridge and much of central Sydney's
  twentieth-century infrastructure" is the safe register.
- The Sydney Opera House claim. **Do not use in copy until R3
  surfaces a primary source.** Engineering Heritage Australia's
  authoritative Opera House page does not name a cement supplier.
  [S10]
- Implying the site is heritage-listed. It is not. [S10]
- Implying Kandos is dead. It is post-industrial but culturally
  active (Cementa Festival) and may have a methanol-plant future
  (Cenagen). The story is open, not closed. [S13][S11]
