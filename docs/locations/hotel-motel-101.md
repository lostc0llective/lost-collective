# Hotel Motel 101

**Purpose of this file.** Single source of truth for verified historical
facts about Hotel Motel 101. Every claim carries a source reference and
an authority tier. Nothing in Lost Collective copy about Hotel Motel 101
ships unless it is in this file or added here with a source first.

**Rule.** If you cannot find it in this file, do not write it. If you
learn something new, add it here (with source and tier) before you use
it. Unverified items go in the "Claims to verify" block at the bottom,
never into live copy.

**Note on scope.** Hotel Motel 101 is not a single location. It is a
photographic series by Brett Patman covering Australian roadside
motels in NSW. The title is a project conceit: Brett actually
photographed 102 motels and visited approximately 120. This file
documents the series itself rather than a single building.

## The series in detail

### Origin

- Brett sat on the idea for years before starting. [S2]
- Trigger: watching the television series Mindhunter, in which
  characters move through different motel rooms over the course of an
  episode. [S2]
- The 101 figure was a project framing chosen as the shoot progressed:
  "I didn't intend for the collection to be this big. Before I knew
  it, I was at 70 and I was hooked. I thought that as I'd got that
  far, I may as well reach 100, plus one more just to be sure it was
  enough." [S7]
- Brett's only personal stay in any motel he photographed was
  approximately 1988 (thirty years before the BROAD interview in July
  2018). [S2]

### Method

- Motels found by Google Maps searches by direction. [S2]
- Three runs out of Sydney: North along the Central Coast, West via
  the Blue Mountains to the Central Western Tablelands, South down
  the start of the NSW South Coast. First run was the Hume Highway.
  [S3]
- Inclusion criteria: traditional motor inn typology only. Drive-thru
  reception, parking in front of rooms, low rise. Chain motels
  skipped because they "lacked the character... the tacky statues,
  mismatched smokers' chairs in front of the rooms, the pastel paint
  jobs." [S3]
- Approximately 90% of motels visited were photographed; reasons for
  skipping included darkness, parked cars, and people present. [S3]
- Late-night and very-early-morning shooting to avoid people. [S2][S3]
- Anecdotes from the shoot: "more than half" of motels were close to
  capacity on weeknights, with people walking dogs, washing cars at
  3:30 AM, gathering at petrol-station kebab stands. [S7]
- One on-site security incident: a guard repeatedly told Brett "this
  is private property" while Brett protested he had only photographed
  the sign and was leaving. [S2]

### Output

- 102 motels actually captured. ~120 visited. [S3]
- Approximately 26 named motels appear as individual prints on the
  Lost Collective store. The full series is not sold individually.
  [S1]
- A Suitcase Magazine gallery contains 24 selected images. [S7]

## Notable connections

### Thirroul Beach Motel and Brett Whiteley

The Thirroul Beach Motel is where the Australian artist Brett
Whiteley died of a heroin overdose on 15 June 1992. He died in room 4
of the motel. **Room 4 appears in Brett Patman's photograph of the
motel.** Brett Patman has stated he learned this fact only after
publishing the image: "It is one of my favourites because I had two
attempts to get it. I learned later that it was where Brett Whiteley
died of a heroin overdose. The room, number four, appears in the
photo." [S4]

This is the strongest art-historical anchor in the series. Use with
care: Whiteley's death is both publicly documented and a sensitive
subject. The fact stands on Patman's quote in Broadsheet [S4] and on
the well-documented record of Whiteley's death at the Thirroul Beach
Motel in 1992.

## Australian roadside motel typology — institutional context

This section provides the building-type background to support copy
that engages with motels as a type, not just as individual subjects.

### Origins

- Australia's earliest motels predate the post-war boom: the West End
  Motel, Ballina, NSW (1937) and the Penzance Motel, Eagle Hawk,
  Tasmania (1939). [S9]
- The first Australian motel of the modern type is widely credited as
  the **Bellair Drive-in Motel, Orange, NSW**, opened in **1955** by
  **Hugh McCarron** (often called "Father of the Australian Motel").
  Inspired by McCarron's 1952 trip to Death Valley. McCarron then
  opened Australia's first air-conditioned motel in Parkes, ran the
  Caravilla chain, drove the merger that created Travelodge
  Australia, and later operated the Koala chain (from 1962) and a
  half-share in Trans World Inns / Ramada (1970s). The original
  Bellair still operates today as the **Mid City Motor Lodge,
  Orange.** [S8]

### Peak and decline

- Industry peak years for new motel build: **1975 to 1985.** [S8]
- Decade-by-decade amenity progression: 1950s = private bathroom +
  onsite parking; 1960s = colour TVs + swimming pools; 1970s = spa
  baths; 1980s = waterbeds. Bankcard arrived in 1978 and met
  consumer resistance. [S8]
- Decline drivers: budget / no-frills airline travel made flying
  cheaper than driving city-to-city; mobile phones and online travel
  agencies replaced wayfinding signage from the 1990s onwards;
  star-ratings displaced by online reviews; the cooked breakfast
  hatch (~70% margin for operators) became obsolete. [S8]

### Architecture and layout

- Standard typology: I-shaped, L-shaped, or U-shaped layout; guest
  rooms with attached manager's office and small reception; sometimes
  a diner and pool; single-storey with rooms opening directly onto
  the parking lot; second storeys (where present) on a balcony with
  external stairwells; eye-catching neon signs were a 1950s to late
  1960s feature. [S9]

### Heritage status of the typology

- The principal heritage-listed Australian motel is the **Oakleigh
  Motel, Oakleigh, Victoria.** Built 1956 to 1957 (architect James
  Miller, commissioned by Cyril Lewis) for the 1956 Melbourne
  Olympics. Googie / 1950s modernism. Listed on the Victorian
  Heritage Register (H2193) in 2009 after a National Trust campaign.
  Partly demolished circa 2008 to 2010 for apartments, with walls
  retained. [S10]
- Major institutional engagement with the Australian motel as a
  building type: **National Archives of Australia exhibition
  "Reception this way: motels - a sentimental journey with Tim
  Ross,"** Canberra, 27 May to 4 September 2022. Toured to Orange
  Regional Museum, Museum of the Great Southern (WA), and others.
  [S11]

This wider context lets Lost Collective copy frame Hotel Motel 101 as
a contemporary record of a building type that is both genuinely
endangered (Airbnb pressure, decline drivers above) and starting to
attract serious heritage and institutional attention (Oakleigh VHR
listing, NAA national exhibition).

## Things worth noting in photographs

- Neon and backlit signage; vacancy / no-vacancy signs. [S2][S3]
- Mid-century and late twentieth century motel architecture: drive-thru
  reception, single-storey strips, L-shaped or U-shaped courts, parking
  in front of each room. [S3]
- Pastel paint jobs, tacky statues, mismatched smokers' chairs in
  front of rooms; "red-bricked walls, jarring colour schemes and
  kitsch palm tree planters." [S3][S4]
- Contrast of lit signage against dark sky. [S2][S3]
- Specific captions with story attached:
  - The Grandstand Motel sign at Warwick Farm with its layered pricing.
    [S2]
  - Room 4 at the Thirroul Beach Motel. [S4]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back
to at least one verified fact.

1. **102, not 101.** The title is a project conceit. The real number
   is 102 photographed and ~120 visited. [S3] Brett's own framing of
   how the count crept up from 70 toward 100, plus one for safety, is
   the canonical origin story. [S7]
2. **Three runs.** North to the Central Coast, west via the Blue
   Mountains to the Central Western Tablelands, south down the South
   Coast. The Hume Highway was the first run. [S3]
3. **Night photography of a disappearing typology.** Shot late at
   night and very early in the morning. The series captures motels
   under their own signage, with no people. [S2][S3]
4. **Mindhunter as trigger.** A specific, citable creative origin
   story. [S2]
5. **Airbnb pressure on roadside motels.** Contextual framing Patman
   himself used. [S2] Now corroborated by the broader industry account
   (mobile phones, OTAs, budget air travel, star-ratings displaced by
   reviews). [S8]
6. **Full spectrum.** Pastel beachside boutiques through to seedy and
   rundown. Inclusion criteria deliberately excluded chains. [S3]
7. **Where Brett Whiteley died.** Room 4, Thirroul Beach Motel, 15
   June 1992. The room appears in the photograph. [S4] The strongest
   art-historical hook in the series.
8. **The first Australian motel still stands.** Bellair Drive-in
   Motel, Orange (1955), now Mid City Motor Lodge. [S8] Useful for
   any piece that wants to bookend Hotel Motel 101 against the origin
   point of the building type.
9. **Heritage attention catching up.** Oakleigh Motel VHR listing
   (2009), NAA national exhibition (2022). [S10][S11] The motel as a
   typology is moving from "everyday building" to "subject worth
   preserving" in real time.

**Angles to avoid.** Do not describe Hotel Motel 101 as a single
"hotel" or "motel". Do not imply the buildings are all abandoned (most
are still operating). Do not invent specific motel names, locations,
or anecdotes that are not in this file or in the source articles. Do
not lean on the Whiteley connection in lightweight contexts (social
sales captions, throwaway alt text); it is a major hook and deserves
the right surface.

## Wording to use

- "Series" or "collection." Not "a motel" or "the motel." Hotel Motel
  101 is plural in its subject.
- "102 motels" or "102 captures" when accuracy matters more than the
  title. "Hotel Motel 101" when the project name is the subject.
- "Motor inn" is acceptable and historically accurate as an Australian
  term for the typology. [S3]
- "Roadside motel" is the typology phrase preferred by ResortBrokers,
  by Tim Ross's NAA exhibition, and broadly in Australian heritage
  writing. [S8][S11]
- For Thirroul Beach Motel specifically: "the motel where Brett
  Whiteley died" is accurate and direct. "Where Brett Whiteley spent
  his last hours" is also accurate.
