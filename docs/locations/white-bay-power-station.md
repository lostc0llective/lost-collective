## Chronology

- **1910.** Need for a new Sydney power house first officially mooted, to
  cope with increasing use and expansion of the electric tram system. The
  Railway Commissioners also intended to electrify the suburban rail
  network and build an underground system (the future City Circle).
  [S6]
- **June 1912.** Construction begins on the first stage. [S6]
- **3 July 1913.** First day of operation. NSW State Archives holds a
  Railway Department photograph captioned "first day of operation",
  archive reference 0363-004. [S10] Boilers and alternators were run
  before the buildings that housed them were complete. [S1][S6]
- **1914.** Third Dick, Kerr alternator arrives from England but delayed
  delivery means it is installed at Ultimo Power Station first. [S1]
- **May 1917.** Station finally comes on line. Delayed from earlier target
  by the outbreak of World War I. [S6]
- **1917.** Second Dick, Kerr alternator set installed at White Bay. [S1]
- **1918.** Third Dick, Kerr alternator transferred from Ultimo to White
  Bay as unit No. 3, giving the station an initial capacity of 28.5 MW.
  [S1]
- **1923 to 1928.** B Station expansion, the first of two major updating
  phases. [S6][S10]
- **1925.** Turbine Hall photographed by Railway Department, archive
  reference 0363-083 (NSW State Archives). [S10]
- **1928.** 20 MW Parsons unit (No. 9) enters service, bringing B Station
  to 86 MW and the site total to 144.5 MW. [S1]
- **1945 to 1948.** C Station expansion and final works phase per the EHA
  nomination. [S10] The Dictionary of Sydney framing of the second
  updating as "1945 to 1953" appears to conflate the works window
  (1945 to 1948) with the separate January 1953 transfer of operational
  control to the Electricity Commission of NSW. [S6][S10]
- **1948.** Final works completed. [S10]
- **1949.** Newly completed control room photographed, Electricity
  Commission archive (NSW State Archives). [S10]
- **1954.** Boiler house, chimney, coal conveyor and ash plant
  photographed, Electricity Commission archive (NSW State Archives). [S10]
- **1956.** Nos. 1 and 2 Parsons turbines photographed, Electricity
  Commission archive reference 00937 (NSW State Archives). [S10]
- **Third stage (C Station).** Adds 100 MW, bringing theoretical maximum
  to 186 MW (rarely attained). [S1] Exact unit-by-unit commissioning
  dates within the 1945 to 1948 window: see Claims to verify.
- **January 1953.** White Bay brought under the control of the new
  Electricity Commission of New South Wales, alongside Ultimo and regional
  stations at Newcastle and Lithgow. [S6]
- **1956.** Power supply amalgamation under the Electricity Commission
  completed (ECNSW established 1950). [S6]
- **After 1958.** Station supplied power to the wider NSW electricity
  grid (previously dedicated to the tram and rail network). [S10]
- **1975.** Units 6 to 9 decommissioned and removed. Only the 50 MW Parsons
  units remain in service. [S1]
- **1982.** Last period of intensive use during Sydney power shortages.
  [S1]
- **25 December 1983.** Production ceases. Final shutdown on Christmas
  Day. [S1]
- **1984.** Formal decommissioning. [S1][S6]
- **1990s.** Site decontaminated, asbestos removed, majority of remaining
  machinery taken away. [S1]
- **2 April 1999.** Listed on NSW State Heritage Register. [S4][S10]
  Note: the Dictionary of Sydney entry (2008) gives the listing year as
  2006; 1999 is the authoritative date per the Register itself. [S6]
  Listing number: see Claims to verify (HMS record [S4] cites 01025;
  EHA nomination [S10] cites H01015).
- **May 2003.** Godden Mackay Logan completes *White Bay Power Station
  Historic Machinery Inventory and Conservation Strategies* for the
  Sydney Harbour Foreshore Authority. [S6][S9]
- **2003.** Filming of The Matrix Reloaded. A metal staircase erected in
  the boilerhouse during the shoot still stands. [S7]
- **2012.** Filming of The Great Gatsby; set re-used infrastructure from
  The Matrix Reloaded. [S7]
- **2020s.** Placemaking NSW delivers remediation and conservation works
  to repurpose the site as an arts, cultural and community venue. [S5]
- **9 March to 10 June 2024.** 24th Biennale of Sydney opens White Bay
  Power Station to the public for the first time in over 100 years.
  [S5][S8]
- **2026.** 25th Biennale of Sydney ("Rememory") returns to the venue.
  [S5]

## Architecture and site

- Site area 38,000 square metres on the western edge of White Bay,
  Rozelle. [S1][S10]
- Built in three phases across A, B, and C Stations. [S1][S2][S6][S10]
- **Designer and builder.** NSW Department of Railways, Railways
  Commissioners Department, listed as both designer and builder in the
  EHA nomination. [S10] No individual architect is recorded against the
  site. Resolves the "Architect(s)" claim to verify.
- **Architectural style.** Three published descriptors disagree.
  Wikipedia: Federation Anglo-Dutch [S1]. CATO Location Services:
  Federation Free Style [S7]. EHA nomination: "rare use of the Arts
  and Crafts design in an industrial complex" [S10]. Conservation
  Management Plan (S3) is the authoritative arbiter. See Claims to
  verify.
- Dictionary of Sydney describes the station as occupying a dominant
  position on the western edge of the city, with boiler houses presenting
  a "dramatic vertical façade, castellated parapets and steam funnels
  which extended upwards and faced out to the harbour and the city."
  [S6]
- Described by Mark Dunn (Dictionary of Sydney, 2008) as "cathedral-like
  in their industrial scale," referring specifically to the boiler
  houses and turbine rooms. [S6] Note: this is a published historian's
  descriptor, not Brett's. Use as a quoted framing only if attributed.
- Striking red brick and sandstone facade. [S7]
- The original boilerhouse (singular) on A Station housed four Babcock
  and Wilcox boilers reaching 30 metres to the roof. [S1]
- Remaining chimneys still visible from many vantage points across the
  harbour. [S6]
- Turbine hall, control room, and switchboards remain. [S1][S4]

**Wording to use.** "Boilerhouse" is the correct form (one boilerhouse,
not multiple). Turbine halls (plural) is correct because the site had
multiple turbine halls across A, B and C Stations.

**Site selection rationale** (from Dictionary of Sydney [S6]):
- Rail and dock access on site for coal delivery, plant delivery, and ash
  disposal.
- Unlimited circulating water (harbour).
- Low-lying topography, which reduced the amount of lift required to move
  cooling water through the plant.
- Cost: relatively cheap land compared with alternative sites considered
  outside Sydney.

## Machinery and operations

- **Boilers.** 8 Babcock & Wilcox boilers, each producing 30,000 lb/hour
  of steam at 205 psi and 588 °F. [S1]
- **Original boilers.** Four towering Babcock and Wilcox boilers up to 30
  metres high in the boilerhouse. [S1]
- **Early alternators.** Three 750 rpm, 25 Hz turbo-alternators from
  Willans & Robinson of Rugby and Dick, Kerr & Co. of Preston, England.
  Continuous rating 8.7 MW; two-hour rating 10.5 MW. [S1]
- **Later turbines.** Parsons turbines from 1928 onward (20 MW units)
  and later 50 MW Parsons units (the surviving longest). [S1]
- **Fuel.** Coal-fired. [S1]
- **Network role.** White Bay and Ultimo Power Stations together formed
  the tramway and railway supply grid, linked by a 6,600 V transmission
  network feeding railway and tramway sub-feeder stations. Initial DC
  output was 600 V; Ultimo supplied three-phase AC at 6,600 V, 25 cycles
  per second. [S2]
- **Heritage significance of surviving machinery.** Only power station in
  NSW to retain in situ a full set of both structures and machinery from
  the early to mid 20th century. [S4]
- **Surviving machinery inventory.** Documented in Godden Mackay Logan's
  *White Bay Power Station Historic Machinery Inventory and Conservation
  Strategies*, May 2003, prepared for the Sydney Harbour Foreshore
  Authority. [S9] This is the definitive machinery reference.

## Heritage status

- **NSW State Heritage Register,** listed 2 April 1999. [S4][S10]
  Listing number is recorded as 01025 in the HMS record cited by S4 and
  as H01015 in the EHA nomination [S10]. See Claims to verify. Until
  resolved, prefer phrasing that avoids the numeric listing code in
  live copy.
- **Listing rationale (paraphrased from S4):**
  - Important in demonstrating the course or pattern of NSW cultural and
    industrial history, particularly the state's development of electrical
    power for industry and growth during the first 70 years of the 20th
    century.
  - Strong association with cultural history through rare ability to
    demonstrate once common and standard work practices of the early to
    middle 20th century now discontinued through changes in technology
    and occupational health and safety.
  - Principal characteristics of a class. Retains a complete system of
    steam turbine generation of electricity from burning of fossil fuel,
    highly representative of this generation of power station.

## Role in Sydney's network

- Supplied electricity for Sydney's expanding tram and suburban rail
  network. [S1][S2][S6]
- Was the longest-serving Sydney power station operating from the same
  building. [S6]
- At the time of construction, Sydney had three independent electricity
  suppliers, each running its own stations: the Railway Commissioners,
  the Sydney Municipal Council, and the Electric Light and Power Supply
  Corporation. White Bay was the Railway Commissioners' station. The
  three systems were not amalgamated until the creation of the
  Electricity Commission of New South Wales in 1950, with full
  amalgamation completed in 1956. [S6]
- Powered the largest metropolitan tramway network in the Southern
  Hemisphere: see Claims to verify for a Tier 1 source on the "largest in
  Southern Hemisphere" claim.

## Redevelopment and current use

- Part of the Bays West urban renewal precinct. [S5]
- Remediation and conservation delivered by Placemaking NSW. [S5]
- Opened to the public for the 24th Biennale of Sydney, 9 March to 10
  June 2024. First public opening since the station closed. [S5][S8]
- Hosted the 25th Biennale of Sydney in 2026 under the title "Rememory".
  [S5]
- Positioned as a long-term arts, culture and community venue. [S5]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each has at least one
verified fact behind it.

1. **Longest-serving metropolitan power station.** 66 years of production.
   [S1]
2. **Last complete set of its kind.** Only NSW power station retaining in
   situ structures and machinery from the period. [S4]
3. **Powered the trams.** Built specifically for the tram and rail
   network, not general electricity supply. [S1][S2]
4. **Shut on Christmas Day 1983.** Unusually precise and quietly striking
   detail. [S1][S6]
5. **A site built in phases.** A, B, and C Stations layered over decades.
   Machinery from multiple eras sits beside each other. [S1][S2]
6. **Restoration and reopening.** From industrial ruin to the centrepiece
   of Bays West, reopened for the Biennale in 2024 after being closed to
   the public for the entire life and afterlife of the site. [S5]
7. **Film ghosts.** The Matrix staircase is still there. [S7]

**Angles to avoid.** "Frozen in time." "Echoes of the past." Anything
implying the station is an untouched time capsule. It is not: the site
was decontaminated and had most of its remaining machinery removed during
the 1990s. What survives is significant but partial.
