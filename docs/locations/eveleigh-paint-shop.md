## Chronology

- **1856 to 1899.** John Whitton serves as Engineer-in-Chief for NSW Railways; conceives the consolidated Eveleigh workshops. [S6]
- **1878.** The Eveleigh site is resumed for construction of the Railway Workshops. [S1]
- **1878.** George Cowdery appointed chief assistant to William Mason, Engineer-in-Chief for Existing Lines. [S7]
- **1880.** Cowdery succeeds Mason as Engineer-in-Chief for Existing Lines; holds the post for 10 years. [S7]
- **1882 to 1887.** Eveleigh Railway Workshops built to the designs of NSW Government Railways Chief Engineer George Cowdery. [S1]
- **1884.** George Fishburn holds the contract for the initial Locomotive Workshops bays (south side). Not explicitly named against the Paint Shop build. [S6]
- **1884.** Fan of Tracks (N12) built against the north-east elevation of the Paint Shop. [S6]
- **1887.** Brick main wing of the Paint Shop completed. [S6][S2]
- **c.1888.** Carriage and Wagon Workshops and Paint Shop described in the SHR timeline as built. [S6]
- **1890.** Cowdery retires at statutory age from NSWGR. [S7]
- **1901.** Traverser No. 1 (N24) installed between the Paint Shop and the Carriage Shops. [S6]
- **1908.** Eveleigh begins manufacturing steam locomotives; site employment grows beyond 3,000. [S1]
- **1912.** Suburban Car Workshops iron-clad extension added to the Paint Shop. [S2] (SHR timeline gives 1913 for "Paint Shop extension added". [S6])
- **27 March 1913.** George Cowdery dies at Blackheath, NSW, aged 82. [S7]
- **1971.** Traverser No. 1 trolley replaced. [S6]
- **End of 1987.** Locomotive Workshops (south side) close. [S6]
- **1989.** Suburban Car Workshops of the Carriage Works (the 1912 Paint Shop extension) close. [S6]
- **1990.** All railway operations at the Locomotive and Carriage Workshops cease. [S6]
- **2 April 1999.** Eveleigh Railway Workshops listed on the NSW State Heritage Register (item 01141), NSW Government Gazette No. 27, p. 1546. [S6]
- **15 December 2023.** Rezoning of the Paint Shop precinct commences under an amendment to the Sydney Local Environmental Plan 2012, including a provision requiring 15 per cent of residential floor space to be used for affordable housing. [S4]

## Architecture and site

- Large single-storey building. Main wing (1887): eight rail roads in the brick section, each separated by a single row of cast iron columns supporting a sawtooth south-light roof. [S6]
- Adjacent metal-clad section (1912 extension, the Suburban Car Workshops / Paint Shop extension, SHR item N16): steel-framed, corrugated iron clad, concrete slab floor, timber-framed windows to the north façade. Shares its southern wall with the 1887 brick main wing. Five rail roads. [S6]
- South-light sawtooth roof glazing is characteristic of nineteenth-century industrial architecture, giving consistent north-facing light into the work bays.
- Located on the north side of the Sydney main railway line. South side holds the Locomotive Workshops, Running Sheds and Manager's Office. North side (Carriage Works) holds the Carriage Workshops (N3), Paint Shop (N4), Suburban Car Workshops (N16), Chief Mechanical Engineers Office (N6), Fan of Tracks (N12), Traverser No. 1 (N24) and Traverser No. 2 (N25). [S6]

**Wording to use.** "Paint Shop" (two words, capitalised) is the standard form used in heritage listings and the current precinct planning documents. "Carriage Works" is used for the broader group of buildings the Paint Shop sits within.

## Machinery and operations

- Purpose-built for the specialist task of painting and finishing railway carriages. [S1]
- Originally supported the wider NSW Government Railways carriage fleet produced and maintained at Eveleigh. [S1]
- The SHR listing states that the most concentrated surviving machinery collection sits in the Australian Technology Park (ATP, south side) buildings, not the Paint Shop. [S6]
- Pre-redevelopment use: owned by RailCorp, used by the NSW Office of Rail Heritage to store heritage rolling stock and moveable relics. [S2]
- A Moveable Heritage Management Strategy for Eveleigh has been submitted to Heritage NSW for endorsement, covering the surviving machinery and moveable items across the precinct. [S8]

## Heritage status

- NSW State Heritage Register, item **01141**, Eveleigh Railway Workshops. Gazetted 2 April 1999, NSW Government Gazette No. 27, p. 1546. [S6]
- Separate SHR item **5001063** covers Eveleigh Railway Workshops machinery. The curtilage for this item is the items themselves, not the buildings housing them. [S8]
- The Paint Shop (item code N4 within the listing) is graded **Exceptional Significance**, the highest grading in the North Eveleigh precinct. [S6]
- The 1912 Suburban Car Workshops / Paint Shop Extension (item code N16) is graded **Moderate Significance**. [S6]
- The Eveleigh Railway Workshops are described in the SHR listing and in [S1] as of national cultural significance as part of the largest intact, high quality workshop site from the steam era in Australia.
- The significance assessment in the SHR listing is compiled from the Eveleigh Carriageworks Draft CMP (Otto Cserhalmi & Partners, 2002), the South Eveleigh Heritage Assessment 2015, and the North Eveleigh Concept Plan Heritage Impact Statement 2014. [S6]

## Role in wider context

- The Eveleigh Railway Workshops were the principal servicing, repair and later manufacturing facility for the NSW rail fleet from the steam era. [S1]
- Manufactured more than 3,000 locomotives across the site's operating life. Employed more than 3,000 people by 1908. [S1]
- The Paint Shop specifically served the Carriage Works side of the site, responsible for the finishing of passenger and goods rolling stock. [S1]
- Final closure of Paint Shop operations in 1989 and total precinct closure in 1990 marks the end of integrated state-run heavy rail manufacturing in inner Sydney. [S6]

## People

- **John Whitton.** Engineer-in-Chief for NSW Railways, 1856 to 1899. Conceived the Eveleigh workshops scheme. [S6]
- **George Cowdery.** Engineer-in-Chief for Existing Lines, NSWGR, 1880 to 1890. Executed Whitton's vision at Eveleigh. Born Portsea, Hampshire, c.1830. Worked on the Britannia Bridge at the Menai Straits with Robert Stephenson in 1847; drew plans for the Lowestoft Pier lighthouse in 1848 for Sir Morton Peto. Appointed to NSWGR in 1863 by Whitton as district engineer on the Great Southern Railway, supervising NSW's first railway tunnels at Picton and Mittagong. Later district engineer on the Great Western Line including the Zig Zag. Chief assistant to William Mason from 1878; succeeded Mason in 1880. Retired 1890 at statutory age. Died at Blackheath, NSW, 27 March 1913, aged 82. [S7] (SHR listing [S6] uses the spelling "Cowdrey"; Trove obituary [S7] and eveleighstories.com.au [S2] use "Cowdery".)
- **William Mason.** Engineer-in-Chief for Existing Lines to 1880, preceding Cowdery. [S7]
- **George Fishburn.** Held the contract for the initial Locomotive Workshops bays in 1884. [S6]

## Redevelopment and current use

- Part of the Redfern North Eveleigh Precinct Renewal led by Transport for NSW. [S3]
- Paint Shop Sub-Precinct of approximately 6,000 m² (sub-precinct, not building footprint). Planned to accommodate around 700 residents and 6,500 workers on completion. [S4]
- Plans include approximately 450 new homes across 16 new and refurbished buildings up to 28 storeys, with industrial heritage items including the Paint Shop adaptively reused for cultural and employment uses. [S4]
- Rezoning commenced 15 December 2023. [S4]
- National Trust of Australia (NSW) lodged an August 2022 submission on the proposed Paint Shop Sub-Precinct. [S5]
- At R2, redevelopment is in progress, not complete. Current state of the Paint Shop fabric to be confirmed before use in copy.

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back to at least one verified fact.

1. **Steam-era scale.** Eveleigh is the largest intact, high quality workshop site from the Australian steam era. The Paint Shop (N4) sits in the North Eveleigh Carriage Works, graded "Exceptional Significance" by the NSW State Heritage Register. [S1][S6]
2. **A century of carriage finishing.** 1887 to 1989, a hundred years of painting and finishing NSW's rolling stock in one building, across a brick main wing and a 1912 iron-clad extension. [S6]
3. **North-light industrial architecture.** Sawtooth south-light roof and cast iron columns define the volume and the quality of light inside. [S6]
4. **Whitton's vision, Cowdery's hand.** Conceived by Engineer-in-Chief John Whitton, executed by Engineer-in-Chief for Existing Lines George Cowdery, the same engineer responsible for NSW's first railway tunnels at Picton and Mittagong. [S6][S7]
5. **End of inner Sydney heavy manufacturing.** Closure in 1989, with the rest of the precinct shutting down by 1990, marks a clean break in the city's industrial fabric. [S6]
6. **Heritage in transition.** The Paint Shop sits at the centre of one of the largest current precinct renewals in Sydney, anchored by its N4 "Exceptional Significance" grading. [S3][S4][S6]

**Angles to avoid.** Do not conflate the Paint Shop with the Locomotive Workshops (south of the line, now occupied by other tenants including Carriageworks and the South Eveleigh tech precinct). The Paint Shop is a distinct building with a distinct history. Do not use the phrase "world heritage significance per Smithsonian curators" without a primary source (see Claims to verify).

## Things worth noting in photographs

- The sawtooth south-light roof and the rhythm of the cast iron column rows in the 1887 brick main wing. [S6]
- The contrast between the eight-road brick main wing (1887) and the five-road metal-clad Suburban Car Workshops extension (1912). [S6]
- Paint residues, marks and accumulated surfaces on the floor and walls tied to a century of carriage finishing work. Specific features to be confirmed from Otto Cserhalmi & Partners 2002 Draft CMP in R3.
- Traverser No. 1 (1901, trolley replaced 1971), the six-rail transfer table between the Paint Shop and Carriage Shops. [S6]
- Fan of Tracks (1884) against the north-east elevation, the rail connection into the rest of the precinct. [S6]
- Scale: the building is large enough to hold thirteen roads of full-length passenger carriages across the two sections.
