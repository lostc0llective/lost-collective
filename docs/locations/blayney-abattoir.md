## Chronology

- **By 1900.** A butter factory and freezing works occupied the site, employing a significant proportion of Blayney's working population. [S2]
- **1957.** Site converted to and began operating as an abattoir. [S1][S2]
- **1996.** ANZCO Foods Limited (New Zealand based meat processor) acquired the facility. [S2]
- **March 1998.** ANZCO announces closure citing stock shortage. Operations scale back rapidly. The Sydney Morning Herald reported the closure on 10 March 1998 noting that approximately 600 workers were given a week's pay. The AMIEU (Australian Meat Industry Employees' Union) cited economic factors rather than stock availability as the main cause; trade press noted ANZCO was carrying large unsold stock as a result of the Asian financial crisis. [S2]
- **1998 to 1999.** Final shutdown completed. The 1998 / 1999 ambiguity in the public record reflects the overlap between operational cessation (1998) and full closure of remaining functions (into 1999). [S1][S2]

## Architecture and site

- **Multi-floor processing layout** typical of mid-century Australian abattoir construction:
  - Mutton floor with two chains; the larger chain processed 3,000 to 4,000 sheep a day, the smaller around 1,500. [S2]
  - Beef floor processing 250 to 400 cattle a day. [S2]
  - Pig floor processing approximately 400 pigs a day. [S2]
- **Cold stores, holding pens, slaughter floor, offal rooms, boning rooms, packing rooms** — all standard for an abattoir of this scale.
- **Site has stood disused since 1998 / 1999** and has accumulated decay, weather damage, and intermittent vandalism characteristic of long-vacant industrial sites in regional NSW. [S4]

---

## Operations

- **Peak processing capacity.** Combined daily throughput across the three species at peak operation: approximately 5,500 sheep, 350 cattle, and 400 pigs. [S2]
- **Markets.** Domestic and export. ANZCO's Australian operations were a significant link in NZ–Australia trans-Tasman meat industry consolidation through the 1990s. [S2]

## Role in wider context

- **A regional employer of substantial scale.** With 1,600 employees at peak in a town the size of Blayney, the abattoir was one of the dominant employers in Central West NSW for decades. Its closure had a measurable economic effect on the town. [S2]
- **A representative case of late-1990s Australian meat industry contraction.** The Blayney closure sits inside a wider pattern of regional abattoir consolidation through the 1990s, driven by the Asian financial crisis (which compressed export demand), industry consolidation under NZ-headquartered processors, and changing transport and chilled-supply economics that favoured fewer, larger plants closer to ports. [S2]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back
to at least one verified fact.

1. **Forty-one years of work, then quiet.** The abattoir operated from 1957 to 1998 — just over four decades. [S1][S2]
2. **The end of a town's main job.** 1,600 people, then 100, then nothing. The Blayney closure is the human-scale story of late-1990s regional manufacturing collapse. [S2]
3. **A site that's been there longer than the abattoir.** A butter factory and freezing works occupied the site from at least 1900. The abattoir was the second industrial use, not the first. [S2]
4. **Hard to look at, and hard to look away from.** (Per Brett.) The Blayney photographs are among the most confronting in the Lost Collective archive — and among the most viewed.

**Angles to avoid.** "Sixty years of operation" — wrong; the operational period is approximately 41 years. "Closed in the early 2000s" — wrong; closure was 1998 / 1999. Do not romanticise the building's purpose. Treat the work the building was for with neutrality, not horror or reverence.
