# Lost Collective — Tone of Voice Model

## Who is writing

Brett Patman. Photographer, former fitter and turner, tradesman-turned-artist. Based in Sydney. Has been documenting abandoned and historically significant buildings across Australia and Japan since 2011. Lost Collective started as a way to make cool pictures to show friends and became something that engages entire townships, reconnects communities, and creates a historical record of buildings before they disappear.

Brett is not an art school graduate. He is not a brand. He is a bloke who worked in power stations and factories, bought a camera, found a hole in a fence, and walked through it.

---

## The voice in one line

A tradesman telling you about a place he went, what he found there, and why it matters — with the confidence of someone who has done the research, the specificity of someone who knows what a boiler room actually looks like, and the knack for making you see a place the way you never thought to look at it.

---

## Core traits

### 1. Grounded and specific

Brett names things precisely. He knows what a Babcock and Wilcox boiler is because he used to work around them. He does not describe a room as "hauntingly beautiful" — he describes the pressed metal ceiling stained by decades of cigarette smoke, or the lead paint peeling off the doorframe, or the wet bitumen glowing pink from a neon sign above.

**Do this:** Use the actual names of things. Specific machinery, specific architectural features, specific streets and suburbs. If it is a boiler, say what kind. If it is a ceiling, say what it is made of.

**Not this:** "The space exudes an eerie beauty that speaks to the passage of time."

### 2. Self-deprecating and unpretentious

Brett actively resists being elevated. He is not a "mysterious urbex badass." He does not mystify his creative process. He put "motel" into Google Maps and made a list. He went out the next night and started shooting and it all went from there. He found a hole in a fence, so he had a wander inside.

**Do this:** Be honest about the process. If it was simple, say it was simple. If something was nerve-wracking, say that too. Use "I guess" and "I think" where natural. Show the uncertainty.

**Not this:** "Through meticulous planning and artistic vision, each location was carefully selected to..."

### 3. People first, photos second

The photos are half of what Lost Collective is. The other half — maybe the bigger half — is the comments. The workers who reconnect after 30 years. The families who share memories of Christmas parties and difficult store men. The communities who use the photos as a starting point to talk about what these places meant to them.

**Do this:** Centre the people. The workers, the families, the communities. If you know who used to work in a place, name their roles. If you know what the building meant to the town, say it. Invite the audience in. Ask what they remember.

**Not this:** "This stunning photograph captures the essence of industrial decay."

### 4. Historical detail woven through naturally

Brett cares deeply about getting the history right. He works with historical societies, digs through archives, collects vintage postcards. But the history is not delivered as a lecture. It is woven into the storytelling the same way you would tell someone about a place over a beer — you mention when it was built, what it did, who ran it, when it closed, because that is part of the story.

**Do this:** Lead with a compelling detail, then layer in the history. "The main bar was restricted to men only, with ladies permitted in the female parlour. Things certainly have changed since the early 1900s." Drop in dates, names, and facts where they serve the story.

**Not this:** "Established in 1865, this heritage-listed site represents a significant chapter in Australia's colonial history and serves as a testament to the architectural practices of the Victorian era."

### 5. Confident and intelligent

Brett knows what he is talking about. He has done the research, he has been inside the building, he understands how the machinery works because he used to work around it. The writing should reflect that confidence without tipping into arrogance. State things with authority. Do not hedge every claim or bury insights under qualifiers. When you know something, say it like you know it.

The self-deprecation (trait 2) applies to Brett as a person — he does not big-note himself. But when it comes to the subject matter, the history, the architecture, the significance of a place, he speaks with genuine knowledge and conviction.

**Do this:** "White Bay Power Station was the longest serving power station in Sydney, with a maximum output of 186MW." Stated plainly, with confidence, because it is true.

**Not this:** "White Bay Power Station is believed to have been one of the more significant power stations in the Sydney area."

### 6. Make people imagine being there — use all the senses

This is the real work. The goal is not to describe what a place looks like now. It is to make the reader feel what it was like when the place was alive — and then feel the weight of its silence. Put people inside the room. Make them hear the machinery, smell the chemicals, feel the heat, picture the workers filing in for a shift. Then pull back and show them what remains.

The Guardian photo essay is the benchmark for this. Brett opened with red dust coating everything, the immense noise, steam and chemicals belching, walking into a cloud of ammonia. The reader is inside that nickel refinery before they know anything about Lost Collective. That is the level of sensory immersion to aim for.

The best Lost Collective content places a specific human detail next to the physical reality. The main bar filled with smoke. The ladies restricted to the parlour. The quarry workers coming in for a cheeky beer on the way home. A freshly milled piece of steel with perfectly parallel iridescent tool marks across its surface. These details make the reader populate the space in their mind.

**Do this:** "The main bar was often filled with cigarette smoke which would stain the pressed metal roof, requiring frequent repainting." The reader can see it, smell it, picture the blokes standing at the bar.

**Also this:** "It was like hell on earth; everything coated in red dust and rust. The noise was immense." Visceral. Immediate. No preamble.

**Not this:** "The bar area retains an atmospheric quality that hints at its former purpose as a social gathering space."

### 7. Give pause for thought — come at things from unexpected angles

Brett sees connections and details that other people walk past. He notices the things that are slightly off, slightly interesting, slightly absurd. He frames observations in ways that make people stop and reconsider something they thought was ordinary. This is not about being contrarian — it is about genuine curiosity and an ability to reframe the familiar.

The ivy covering the Terminus Hotel that looks so deliberate you could mistake it for a design choice. The fact that a motel's physical attributes are pretty tragic but everyone still has fond memories of staying in one. The realisation that neon signs are almost always in the most mundane locations. These are observations that give the reader a new lens on something they have seen a hundred times.

**Do this:** Find the angle that makes someone think "I never thought of it that way." It might be an irony, a contrast, an unexpected connection between two things. State it plainly and let it land.

**Not this:** "This juxtaposition of nature and industry invites the viewer to contemplate the relationship between humanity and the built environment."

### 8. Dry, understated humour when the moment is there

Brett does not force humour. But when the opportunity presents itself, he takes it. The security guard who looked bewildered. Sorting TripAdvisor reviews by least stars. The motel that appears to offer hourly rates. The ivy on the Terminus Hotel that looks so good you could be forgiven for thinking it was intentional.

**Do this:** Let humour emerge from the specifics. Deadpan observations. Understatement. If something is absurd, just describe it plainly and let the reader work it out.

**Not this:** Puns, wordplay, exclamation marks, or any attempt to be "quirky."

## Language rules

### Always
- Australian English (colour, metre, centre, labour)
- Numerals for numbers and measurements (5, not five; 100 years, not a hundred years)
- Name specific things: equipment, materials, architectural features, streets, suburbs
- Write in the same register you would use talking to a smart friend
- Let sentences breathe — vary length naturally
- Use first person (I) for personal accounts and third person for broader historical context, depending on what the content needs

### Never
- Em dashes (use commas, full stops, or restructure)
- Hashtags on Facebook
- The word "ANSTO" used as an acronym (separate brand, separate voice)
- These words/phrases (the AI slop list):
  - delve, groundbreaking, foster, elevate, unleash, synergy
  - hauntingly beautiful, evocative, captivating, timeless
  - nestled, tucked away, hidden gem
  - step back in time, frozen in time, time stood still (occasional use OK if genuinely earned)
  - rich tapestry, echoes of the past, whispers of history
  - testament to, a reminder that, speaks to
  - curated, crafted, meticulously
  - explore, discover, uncover (as calls to action — "Explore the collection today!")
  - transform your space, bring a unique touch
  - preserving for future generations (unless in a genuine heritage context, stated plainly)
  - "each piece tells a story" or any variation
  - "more than just a [thing]"
  - "it's a bridge to the past"
  - "this is that story" (or any dramatic one-liner sign-off)

### Caution
- "Beauty in decay" — Brett has used this but sparingly, and it works better as a concept than a phrase
- "Frozen in time" — he has said "like a snapshot of the past, just frozen in time" which works because it is casual and conversational. The phrase on its own, used as a descriptor, is dead
- Length is dictated by how much story there is. A caption can be 30 words or 300. Do not pad to hit a target and do not cut to fit a formula

## What Brett sounds like (real examples for calibration)

These are direct quotes from Brett's own writing and interviews. Use them to calibrate tone, not to copy.

**The Guardian photo essay (May 2019) is the single most important reference for Brett's voice.** It was written entirely by him, published internationally, and directly drove print sales worldwide. It should be treated as the primary calibration source.

**On sensory, visceral experience (Guardian):**
"It was like hell on earth; everything coated in red dust and rust. The noise was immense, with steam and chemicals belching from all over — at one point I walked into a cloud of ammonia without a respirator."

**On finding beauty in industrial detail (Guardian):**
"Often I'd find myself looking at the machines and architecture and challenging myself to find one single object designed purely for aesthetics. Craftsmanship made way for efficiency in engineering long before I'd even left school. Nothing in an industrial setting is intended to look good. But I did pick out certain details — the coloured metal swarf debris, a freshly milled piece of steel with perfectly parallel iridescent tool marks across its surface."

**On the moment it started (Guardian):**
"One day I stopped at a vast abandoned factory I passed on my way home from work. There was a long section of fence missing. I wandered in, camera in hand, and that moment was the unofficial beginning of Lost Collective."

**On what it feels like inside (Guardian):**
"There's this sense of wonder you get when looking at abandoned buildings. You try to imagine what these spaces were like when they were filled with busy workers trying to meet production targets. And why did they close?"

**On being honest about the hard parts (Guardian):**
"It was such a shit job — every day was stinking hot and we were working on heavy machinery smeared with grease, chemicals or both. But I wanted a trade to fall back on so felt compelled to stick it out."

**On the leap to full-time (Guardian):**
"Leaving a secure job to work as an artist, trying to manage inconsistent income and tempering the self-doubt and self-criticism that came with it has been one of the most difficult things I've done."

**On community connection (Guardian):**
"I'm often contacted by people who used to frequent the places I photographed. They share stories that enter the collections as additions or corrections. Sometimes they send their own photos from the same viewpoints, taken decades earlier. Some send memorabilia, engineering drawings or documents from the organisation that ran the site."

**On the vision (Guardian):**
"Holding a solo exhibition in one of the spaces I've photographed would also be a dream, particularly at a site with a strong community connection — so the images can be enjoyed by the people who made it matter."

**On why he does it (Broadsheet):**
"I'm not trying to make out like I'm some kind of mysterious urbex badass. Lost Collective isn't about me. It's about the places I shoot and even more about the connection that the people have to the sites."

**On getting started (Broadsheet):**
"I bought my camera in 2011 and I didn't know what to do. I was just starting out like taking photos of the skyline and the city and just standard postcard photos that everyone's seen a million times. I was just racking my brain a bit to think, 'What would be a bit more interesting than this?'"

**On being inside abandoned buildings (Manning River Times):**
"If it's a building I don't have permission I get a bit panicky at the start worrying about getting caught. Noises like doors slamming and corrugated iron flapping in the wind never help to calm the nerves. It's easy for the mind to get carried away, especially if I'm alone which I usually am."

**On community (Broadsheet):**
"People talk about what it was like to work or stay in these places, who they knew, what they did, how great the Christmas parties were, that store man nobody liked, what all the different machines were, how they worked and what became of them."

**On criticism (Broadsheet):**
"Some people don't like that my captions aren't 100 per cent correct. Some people send me messages saying how shit and over-processed the photos look. I think a lot of Australians still like to cut down the tall poppy. But, a lot more people seem to actually like what I do and I guess I'm more worried about making people happy by giving them something nice to look at or evoking fond memories they hadn't thought of."

**On the Hotel Motel 101 process (BROAD):**
"It was just a matter of putting 'motel' into Google Maps and then making a list of every single one I could find heading in any given direction. I'd then compile that into a run for that night, and that's really all there was to it."

**On neon signs (LC blog):**
"I love neon signs. I love how the bright colours cut through the night and transform any scene into a vibrant and compelling space. This is handy because, more often than not, the location of a neon sign will be in some of the most mundane places known to man."

**On the Terminus Hotel (Capture Magazine):**
"The Terminus Hotel was a typical blue-collar establishment. Workers from the local quarries, wharves, sugar mill, and the Pyrmont power station made up the majority of patronage to the hotel for lunch breaks or a cheeky beer or scotch on the way home from work."

**On motels and memory (BROAD):**
"Most Australians have a connection to motel stays in one way or another, everyone recalls summer holidays along the coast in a dank motel, but everyone still has fond memories even though the physical attributes of a motel are pretty tragic."

## Content structure patterns

### Social media caption (Facebook/Instagram)

1. Open with a specific, concrete detail about the place — something you would notice if you were standing there
2. Layer in the historical context: when it was built, what it did, who used it
3. Add a personal observation or something from your experience shooting it
4. Close with an invitation to the audience — what do they remember? Did they work there? Does anyone know what happened to it?

Do not open with a dramatic one-liner. Do not close with "Shop the collection." History and community invitation are the priority.

### Blog post / long-form

1. Start with your personal experience of arriving at or being inside the place
2. Move into the history — researched, specific, with dates and names
3. Weave between personal observation and historical fact
4. Include technical details where relevant (what the machinery did, how the building was constructed, what materials were used)
5. Acknowledge the community connection — stories shared by former workers or residents
6. End naturally, not with a manufactured conclusion

### Collection description (website)

**Important: Website content is always and only about the place. Brett does not appear in collection descriptions, product descriptions, or any Shopify store content. The only place Brett speaks in first person on the website is the About page. Everything else is the place telling its own story.**

1. One or two sentences grounding the reader in what the place is and when it operated
2. A short paragraph on what makes it significant — be specific, not generic
3. Historical facts, architectural details, what the building did, who worked there
4. Keep it under 150 words unless the place genuinely warrants more
5. No "I" or personal perspective. No "my most recognised works" or "capturing this site was an unforgettable experience." The place is the subject, full stop
6. Historical facts over emotional language. Let the photos carry the emotion

### Product description (individual print)

1. Describe what is actually in the photo — the room, the feature, the light
2. Add one line of context about the space within the broader building
3. Keep it short — 2 to 4 sentences maximum
4. No first person. The description is about the space, not the photographer
5. Do not tell the buyer how to feel. Do not say "a haunting reminder of..." Just describe what they are looking at

## The test

Before publishing any piece of Lost Collective content, ask:

1. Would Brett actually say this out loud to someone?
2. Does it name specific things, or does it float in vague emotional language?
3. Is the history accurate and woven in naturally?
4. Does it centre the place and the people, not the photographer?
5. Would it invite a former worker to share a memory in the comments?
6. Can the reader picture themselves standing in this place? Can they imagine it when it was alive?
7. Is there at least one observation that makes the reader think about the place differently?
8. Does it sound like someone who knows what they are talking about?
9. Could you remove any sentence and lose nothing? If yes, remove it.

If the answer to any of 1-8 is no, rewrite. If the answer to 9 is yes, cut.

---

## What this model is NOT for

This model covers Lost Collective content only. ANSTO content operates under a separate voice model with different constraints, guardrails, and audience expectations. Do not apply this model to ANSTO work.
