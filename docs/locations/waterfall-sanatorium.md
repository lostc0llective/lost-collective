## Chronology

- **14 April 1909.** First patients admitted to the Hospital for
  Consumptives, Waterfall. Initial provision for 180 male patients,
  though two wards were used for staff housing and administration in
  the absence of dedicated buildings for those functions. [S1]
- **May 1912.** New wing for 120 female patients completed.
  Tuberculosis patients transferred from Newington State Hospital on
  its opening. [S1]
- **1912 or 1913.** Renamed Waterfall Sanatorium. [S1][S2]
- **1914.** Waterfall State Sanatorium is the principal NSW hospital
  for the treatment of consumptives, with 370 beds. [S1]
- **1919.** Largest sanatorium in NSW with 788 patients. [S1]
- **Mid 20th century.** Antibiotics (streptomycin and later
  isoniazid) and thoracic surgery transform tuberculosis treatment.
  Isolation of patients in sanatoria becomes unnecessary. [S1]
- **1958.** Waterfall Sanatorium closes. The site converts to
  Garrawarra Hospital. [S1][S3]
- **Later.** Garrawarra Centre for the Aged operates on the site.
  [S1][S3]
- **2019.** Wollongong Council pushes to stop demolition of historic
  buildings at Garrawarra Hospital. [S4]

## Architecture and site

- Elevated bush setting at about 1,000 feet (305 m) above sea level,
  chosen on the medical theory that tuberculosis patients needed "high
  and rarefied atmosphere in the country away from the grime and
  pollution of cities". [S1]
- Sited approximately 26 miles (42 km) south of Sydney, near
  Waterfall on the southern edge of the Royal National Park corridor.
  [S1]
- Fibro chalets, each about the size of a garden shed, used to
  isolate patients in the active phase of tuberculosis. [S1]
- Larger ward buildings for advanced and chronic cases. [S1]
- Administration and staff housing initially occupied wards before
  dedicated buildings were constructed. [S1]

**Wording to use.** "Sanatorium" (Australian English, two As).
"Hospital for Consumptives" is the correct historical name up to 1912
or 1913; "Waterfall Sanatorium" is the correct name from that point
until 1958. "Consumption" was the 19th and early 20th century term for
tuberculosis. "TB" is acceptable shorthand in modern register.

## Buildings and grounds

- Ward buildings for male patients (initial 180, from 1909). [S1]
- Female wing opened May 1912 for 120 patients. [S1]
- Fibro chalets for isolation of active TB cases. [S1]
- Cemetery within the grounds, containing approximately 2,000 graves.
  Controlled by Wollongong Council, though the surrounding land is
  part of the Garrawarra Hospital site. [S1]
- Garrawarra Centre for the Aged buildings, a mix of older Waterfall
  and later additions. [S1][S3]

## Heritage status

- Campaigns in 2019 by Wollongong Council and local heritage advocates
  sought to prevent demolition of historic buildings at Garrawarra.
  [S4]
- Formal heritage listing status to be confirmed at R2: check the NSW
  State Heritage Inventory, the Wollongong LEP, and the Section 170
  register for NSW Health.

## Role in wider context

- Sanatorium-based treatment was the dominant model for tuberculosis
  care in NSW from the late 19th century until the mid 20th century.
  Fresh air, elevation, rest, and isolation were the pillars of
  treatment before antibiotics. Waterfall exemplified this model at
  scale. [S1]
- In 1919 Waterfall was the largest sanatorium in NSW, making it
  the state's central site for long-term TB care. [S1]
- The shift away from sanatoria in the 1950s tracks the arrival of
  effective antibiotic therapy and changing public health strategy.
  [S1]
- NSW State Archives holds records of the Waterfall Hospital for
  Consumptives (1909 to 1912) and Waterfall Sanatorium (1912 to 1958)
  as agency AGY-1995. [S5]

## Redevelopment and current use

- Site now occupied by the Garrawarra Centre for the Aged, operated
  under South Eastern Sydney Local Health District. [S3]
- Some original buildings remain; others demolished or threatened with
  demolition (see S4). [S1][S3][S4]
- Cemetery remains as a distinct feature within the site, controlled
  by Wollongong Council. [S1]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back
to at least one verified fact.

1. **Opened 14 April 1909.** A precise foundation date. [S1]
2. **Largest sanatorium in NSW.** By 1919, 788 patients. [S1]
3. **Fibro chalets the size of garden sheds.** A specific, striking
   detail of the isolation regime. [S1]
4. **Elevation as medicine.** The site was chosen because medical
   orthodoxy of the era said TB needed "high and rarefied atmosphere".
   A quotable framing. [S1]
5. **Two thousand graves.** The scale of 20th century TB mortality is
   written into the site. [S1]
6. **1958 closure as technology pivot.** The sanatorium model ended
   when antibiotics and surgery made isolation unnecessary. [S1]
7. **From sanatorium to aged care.** The site's continuity of care
   across a century, but for a different population. [S1][S3]

**Angles to avoid.** Do not romanticise the isolation chalets or the
cemetery. The site is a working aged care facility and holds 2,000
graves. Keep the register factual and serious. Do not conflate
"Waterfall" with "Garrawarra" as if they were the same institution:
Garrawarra is the post-1958 successor on the same land.

  Not found at R1. Likely a NSW Government Architect's Office project.
- **Names of medical superintendents.** Not found at R1.

  The 2019 demolition threat [S4] suggests the surviving fabric was
  substantial enough to be worth fighting for, but not the full site.
