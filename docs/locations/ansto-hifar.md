## Chronology

- **1950.** Industrial Atomic Energy Policy Committee (precursor body
  to AAEC) established. [S4]
- **15 April 1953.** Atomic Energy Act passed; Australian Atomic
  Energy Commission created. Major-General Sir Jack Stevens appointed
  inaugural Chairman. [S4]
- **7 July 1955.** Head Wrightson Processes Ltd of Stockton-on-Tees,
  UK, awarded the HIFAR contract for £A937,500. [S4]
- **November 1955.** Site clearing begins at the 70-hectare Lucas
  Heights site (Long Bay rifle range at Maroubra had been the
  original candidate; rejected once a high-flux reactor became the
  centrepiece). [S4]
- **June 1956.** Reactor erection begins. Heavy water supplied under
  separate contract from the United States Atomic Energy Commission;
  fuel elements supplied under separate contract from the United
  Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority. [S4]
- **1956.** Australian Institute of Nuclear Science and Engineering
  (AINSE) agreed by government. [S4]
- **1957.** Charles Norman Watson-Munro (AAEC Chief Scientist) takes
  direct charge of HIFAR commissioning. [S4]
- **Late 1957.** Construction completed. [S4]
- **11:15 pm, Sunday 26 January 1958.** HIFAR achieves criticality
  for the first time, with 11 of 25 maximum fuel elements loaded. [S4]
- **18 April 1958.** Research Establishment formally opened, and
  HIFAR set in operation, by Prime Minister R.G. Menzies in front of
  900 staff and invited guests. [S2][S4]
- **4 December 1958.** Inaugural meeting of the AINSE Council. [S4]
- **July 1959.** Mark III fuel introduced (used in mixed core to
  March 1971). [S4]
- **20 October 1960.** Routine 10 MW full-power operations begin,
  following a two-year reactor physics measurement program. [S4]
- **1964.** Mark II fuel last loaded. [S4]
- **November 1970.** Mark IV fuel replaces Mark III; remains in use
  to shutdown. [S4]
- **1973.** Reactor tank flow straighteners installed. [S4]
- **1975.** Downcomer extension installed for emergency core cooling.
  [S4]
- **1982 to 1988.** Major upgrading program. [S4]
- **1987.** AAEC reorganised into ANSTO (Australian Nuclear Science
  and Technology Organisation). [S1]
- **circa 1985.** Commercial silicon irradiation (neutron
  transmutation doping) begins at HIFAR. By 2025, ANSTO marks 40
  years of silicon irradiation. [S4]
- **2000.** ANSTO's technetium-99m generator product line rebranded
  Gentech. [S10]
- **13 July 2000.** OPAL replacement reactor contract awarded to
  INVAP S.E. of Argentina. [S4]
- **5 April 2002.** ARPANSA construction licence issued for OPAL.
  [S4]
- **October 2005.** Department of Environment and Heritage Minister
  announces HIFAR will not be listed on the Commonwealth Heritage
  List, citing public safety and the costs of retaining parts of
  HIFAR for heritage purposes. [S4]
- **End of 2005.** HIFAR has generated 127,800 MWd lifetime energy.
  [S4]
- **20 February 2006.** Glenn Rigden (Chair, Sydney Engineering
  Heritage Committee) recommends approval of the EHA plaquing
  nomination. [S4]
- **14 July 2006.** OPAL operating licence issued. [S4]
- **11:25 pm, 12 August 2006.** OPAL achieves first criticality. [S4]
- **3 November 2006.** OPAL reaches full power. [S4]
- **10:25 am, 30 January 2007.** HIFAR permanently shut down. 49
  years and 4 days of operation. [S2][S8]
- **Within 12 months of shutdown (2007 to 2008).** Spent fuel
  unloaded and reprocessed; heavy water drained and stored on site.
  Reactor fuel and control arms removed. [S2]
- **20 April 2007.** OPAL officially opened by Prime Minister John
  Howard. [S4]
- **15 September 2008.** ARPANSA issues post-shutdown facility
  licence (possess/control). [S2]
- **7 December 2001.** HIFAR awarded National Engineering Landmark
  status by Engineering Heritage Australia. (Note: the EHA register
  records the Award Date as 2001; the formal nomination dossier
  authored by ANSTO is dated 2006, suggesting the 2006 dossier was a
  formal plaquing nomination following the earlier 2001 listing.)
  [S9]
- **4 December 2024.** ARPANSA grants ANSTO's Phase A decommissioning
  facility licence application. [S6]
- **Early 2025.** Phase A Stage 1 decommissioning begins. Removal of
  six neutron beam instruments, two fuel flasks, rig support
  equipment, silicon storage blocks, fuel assembly station, general
  utilisation equipment, and control room. [S2]
- **2026.** Phase A Stage 1 expected complete. [S2]
- **circa 2030.** Phase B (reactor core dismantlement) projected for
  completion, subject to further ARPANSA approvals and a national
  radioactive waste disposal pathway. [S7]

## Architecture and site

- 70-hectare Lucas Heights campus in Sydney's south. The original
  Long Bay rifle range at Maroubra was rejected once the project
  centred on a high-flux reactor. [S4]
- Architects (consulting): **Stephenson and Turner of Sydney.** Major
  Australian practice founded by Sir Arthur Stephenson, with a
  Sydney office from 1934 and 300 to 400 staff at peak. Other
  Stephenson and Turner credits include Qantas House Sydney
  (1949-57, NSW heritage-listed), ACI House Woolloomooloo (1941),
  and ICI House Wellington NZ (1965). They were responsible for the
  HIFAR site's associated buildings and services. [S4][S11]
- Main building contractor: **Hutcherson Bros.** [S4]
- HIFAR reactor block height: 36 ft 6 in (~11.1 m) per Roberts. [S3]
  The R1 figure of "21 metres" likely refers to the containment
  building / dome (the iconic external shell), not the reactor
  itself. Flagged in Claims to verify pending direct confirmation
  from ANSTO documentation or Brett's site knowledge.
- Reactor block: dense iron-shot concrete biological shield,
  steel-clad, right irregular decagonal prism, 6.7 m across the
  flats. ~3,000 tons mass concrete in foundations; 500 tons barytes
  concrete; 100 tons shot concrete. Operating floor 15 ft above
  ground level. [S3]
- External shell: distinctive white containment dome. Retained at
  this stage of decommissioning. [S2]

**Wording to use.**

- HIFAR stands for High Flux Australian Reactor. On first mention use
  the full name with the acronym in brackets, then HIFAR.
- "ANSTO" is the Australian Nuclear Science and Technology
  Organisation; "AAEC" is the earlier Australian Atomic Energy
  Commission. AAEC operated the reactor from commissioning to 1987;
  ANSTO from 1987 to shutdown.
- "Lucas Heights" is the site; the suburb is in Sutherland Shire,
  southern Sydney. Not "Lucas Heights, Sydney's south-west."

## Reactor and operations

- **Class.** DIDO-class. Six DIDO/PLUTO-class reactors built
  worldwide:
  - DIDO (Harwell, UK): first critical November 1956, shut 31 March
    1990. 190,000 MWd lifetime.
  - HIFAR (Lucas Heights): 26 January 1958 to 30 January 2007. **Last
    to cease operation.**
  - PLUTO (Harwell, UK): 27 October 1957 to 31 March 1990.
  - DMTR (Dounreay, Scotland): May 1958.
  - DR-3 (Risø, Denmark): January 1960.
  - FRJ-2 (Jülich, Germany): 1962 to 2006.
  HIFAR was the second of the six built. [S4]
- **Power.** 10 MW thermal. Routine full-power operations from 20
  October 1960. [S3][S4]
- **Coolant and moderator.** Heavy water (D2O), 10 tonnes in primary
  cooling circuit. Heavy water primary flow 285,000 gallons/hr; light
  water secondary 306,000 gallons/hr; ~80,000 gallons/day evaporated
  in cooling towers at 10 MW. Helium gas blanket over the heavy water
  surface to remove radiolytic deuterium and oxygen. [S3][S4]
- **Fuel.** Originally 93% enriched U-235 (Mark II), reduced over
  life to <20% LEU. ~5 lb total U-235 in core (Roberts); 25 fuel
  elements maximum; ~30 elements consumed per year. [S3]
- **Fuel evolution.** Mark II to 1964; Mark III July 1959 to March
  1971 (mixed core); Mark IV from November 1970 to shutdown. [S4]
- **Control.** 6 cadmium "coarse control arms" (semaphore-style),
  1 fine control rod, 2 safety rods clad in stainless steel
  (originally a heavy-water-dump system). [S3]
- **Experimental facilities.** 30 horizontal holes, 28 vertical
  holes. Six neutron beam instruments. Peak thermal neutron flux 1.4
  × 10^14 neutrons/cm²/sec at 10 MW; centre-element power 480 kW;
  centre-element rating 4,300 W/g U-235. [S3]
- **Site power consumption.** ~550 kVA. [S3]

## Heritage status

- **Engineering Heritage Australia, National Engineering Landmark.**
  Awarded 7 December 2001. The EHA register entry lists the award
  date but notes "Plaque/Interpretation Panel: Not available,"
  suggesting no physical plaque was installed. The 2006 nomination
  dossier (authored by ANSTO and recommended for approval by Glenn
  Rigden, Chair of the Sydney Engineering Heritage Committee, on 20
  February 2006) appears to be a formal plaquing dossier following
  the earlier 2001 listing. [S4][S9]
- **NSW State Heritage Register.** Not listed. [S9]
- **Commonwealth Heritage List.** Not listed. The Department of
  Environment and Heritage Minister announced in October 2005 that
  HIFAR would not be listed because of "the need to assure public
  safety and the costs associated with retaining some parts of
  HIFAR for heritage purposes." This was a deliberate ministerial
  non-listing, not an oversight. [S4]
- **Sutherland Shire LEP Schedule 5.** Not confirmed listed. [S9]

## Role in wider context

- **First nuclear reactor built in Australia.** [S1][S2][S4]
- **Built for a nuclear future that did not arrive.** Originally
  intended as a materials research reactor for an Australian civil
  nuclear power programme that was never built. [S4]
- **Reinvention as medicine and semiconductors.** What kept HIFAR
  running for 49 years was not power research but isotope production
  (notably the fission-product technetium-99m generator and Mo-99
  for ~25 years up to 2007), neutron transmutation doping of
  high-purity silicon for the global semiconductor industry, and
  neutron beam science for academic and industrial research. [S4][S10]
- **Tc-99m generators were rebranded Gentech in 2000.** ANSTO has
  supplied Tc-99m generators to 11 Asia-Pacific countries. [S10]
- **Last of the DIDO-class.** One of six DIDO/PLUTO-class reactors
  worldwide, and the last to cease operation. [S4]
- **AINSE.** AAEC's research role was complemented from 1956 by the
  Australian Institute of Nuclear Science and Engineering, with its
  inaugural Council meeting on 4 December 1958. [S4]
- **AAEC at peak.** 1967-68 budget $8.5M; ~1,000 staff including
  ~400 graduates. [S4]
- **The Maralinga era.** AAEC and HIFAR sat firmly on the civil /
  research / industrial-power track, separate from the British
  weapons tests at Emu Field (1953) and Maralinga (1956 to 1963)
  which were a UK-supervised program. The two ran in parallel under
  the same Menzies-era nuclear-ambition political backdrop, but the
  Engineering Heritage Australia dossier explicitly frames HIFAR as
  a peaceful-uses program. Treat them as distinct strands. [S4]
- **Replaced in 2007 by OPAL.** Open Pool Australian Lightwater
  reactor. 20 MW. INVAP S.E. (Argentina) prime contractor with John
  Holland Construction and Evans Deakin Industries as Australian
  alliance partners. Officially opened 20 April 2007 by PM John
  Howard. HIFAR and OPAL ran in parallel briefly until HIFAR's
  shutdown. [S4]

## People

### AAEC and HIFAR foundation team

- **Major-General Sir Jack Stevens.** Inaugural AAEC Chairman, 1953
  to 1956. Formerly Secretary of the Department of Supply. [S4]
- **Sir John Philip Baxter (1905-1989).** Born Machynlleth, North
  Wales. Background: ICI, Tube Alloys, Oak Ridge during WWII. AAEC
  Deputy Chairman from 1953; part-time Chairman from 1957; full-time
  Chairman 1969 to retirement 15 April 1972. UNSW Vice-Chancellor
  1955 to 1969. Later Chairman of the Sydney Opera House Trust 1969
  to 1975. Australia's representative at the IAEA's founding in 1957;
  chaired the IAEA Board of Governors 1969-1970. [S4]
- **Charles Norman Watson-Munro (1915-1991).** New Zealander. AAEC
  Chief Scientist from 1955. Previously worked on ZEEP at Chalk
  River and led the GLEEP team at Harwell. Took direct charge of
  HIFAR commissioning in 1957. Left for Professor of Physics
  (Thermonuclear) at the University of Sydney in 1960. [S4]
- **Dr G.C.J. (Cliff) Dalton.** New Zealander. Rhodes Scholar.
  Deputy Chief Scientist and Chief Engineer of AAEC. Previously
  Professor of Mechanical Engineering at the University of Auckland.
  [S4]
- **Bill (W.H.) Roberts.** Senior engineer. Returned to Australia
  from Harwell to take charge of HIFAR construction. Later
  Operations Manager and Deputy Director at Lucas Heights. Author of
  the foundational 1958 AAEC paper *The Design and Construction of
  HIFAR* [S3]. [S4]
- **George Page.** Worked on uranium enrichment at Oak Ridge during
  the Manhattan Project. Joined AAEC in charge of instrumentation
  and control at HIFAR. Modifications he developed fed back into the
  UK DIDO project. Later head of the Technical Physics Section at
  Lucas Heights, briefly Deputy Director. Died 1967. [S4]
- **Dr H.G. Raggatt FAA.** Foundation AAEC Commissioner. Expert on
  uranium mining. [S4]

### Opening dignitary

- **Sir Robert Menzies, Prime Minister of Australia.** Officially
  opened HIFAR on 18 April 1958 in front of 900 staff and invited
  guests. [S2][S4]

### Decommissioning era

- **Dr Ron Cameron.** Acting Executive Director, ANSTO. Signed the
  2006 Engineering Heritage Australia plaquing nomination. [S4]
- **Glenn Rigden.** Chair, Sydney Engineering Heritage Committee.
  Recommended approval of the 2006 EHA plaquing nomination on 20
  February 2006. [S4]
- **John Howard, Prime Minister of Australia.** Officially opened
  OPAL on 20 April 2007. [S4]

## Redevelopment and current use

- Permanently shut down 30 January 2007 and replaced by OPAL on the
  same site. [S2][S4]
- Spent fuel unloaded and reprocessed within 12 months of shutdown;
  heavy water drained and stored on site. [S2]
- ARPANSA possess/control facility licence issued 15 September 2008.
  [S2]
- ARPANSA Phase A decommissioning facility licence application
  approved 4 December 2024. [S6]
- Phase A Stage 1 began early 2025. Removal of six neutron beam
  instruments, two fuel flasks, rig support equipment, silicon
  storage blocks, fuel assembly station, general utilisation
  equipment, and control room. [S2]
- Phase A Stage 1 expected complete 2026. Phase B (reactor core
  dismantlement) projected complete circa 2030, subject to further
  ARPANSA approvals and a national radioactive waste disposal
  pathway. [S2][S7]
- External shell retained for now. Full dismantlement is not yet
  scheduled. [S2]
- ANSTO has run public Heritage Tours of HIFAR since shutdown and
  currently runs an "HIFAR Community Tour" program. [S12]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back
to at least one verified fact.

1. **Australia's first reactor.** HIFAR ran for 49 years and 4 days,
   from 11:15 pm on 26 January 1958 to 10:25 am on 30 January 2007.
   [S2][S4]
2. **Built for a nuclear future that did not arrive.** Originally
   intended for materials research supporting an Australian civil
   nuclear power programme that was never built. [S4]
3. **Reinvention as medicine and semiconductors.** What kept HIFAR
   running for half a century was the fission-product Tc-99m
   generator, Mo-99 production for nuclear medicine, and silicon
   irradiation for the global semiconductor industry. [S4][S10]
4. **Last of the DIDO-class.** Six DIDO/PLUTO-class reactors built
   worldwide; HIFAR was the last to cease operation. [S4]
5. **The long goodbye.** Spent fuel removed and heavy water drained
   within 12 months of January 2007 shutdown; formal Phase A
   decommissioning licence only issued in December 2024. Seventeen
   years between shutdown and the start of formal Phase A works.
   [S2][S6]
6. **Foundation team from the Manhattan Project.** Several of the
   senior engineers and scientists at HIFAR's foundation had
   experience at Oak Ridge, Chalk River, and Harwell. The reactor
   was designed by people who had been close to the wartime nuclear
   program. [S4]
7. **The white dome.** The retained containment shell is the visible
   landmark of the Lucas Heights skyline. [S2]
8. **Not heritage-listed by choice.** The Commonwealth ministerial
   decision in October 2005 specifically declined Commonwealth
   Heritage listing on safety and decommissioning-cost grounds. The
   Engineering Heritage Australia National Engineering Landmark is
   the only formal recognition. [S4][S9]

**Angles to avoid.**

- Anti-nuclear or pro-nuclear framing in either direction. The fact
  file supports neutral, factual copy only.
- Conflating HIFAR with the British weapons tests at Maralinga.
  Different programs. [S4]
- Implying public access. Lucas Heights is an operational secure
  facility. ANSTO does run public tours but the site is not open in
  the same sense as most Lost Collective subjects.
- Claiming Stephenson and Turner designed the Reserve Bank Sydney.
  They didn't (that's Bates Smart and McCutcheon, 1965). Their
  HIFAR-relevant prestige credits are Qantas House Sydney (NSW
  heritage-listed), ACI House Woolloomooloo, and ICI House Wellington
  NZ. [S11]
