## Chronology

- **1926.** Shimizusawa Thermal Power Plant built by Hokutan to supply coal mines in the Shimizusawa and Yubari area. Initial output around 6,000 kW. [S1][S2]
- **1940.** Shimizusawa Dam constructed to collect and provide cooling water. Dam also operates its own hydroelectric plant generating around 2,000 kW. [S1][S2]
- **Circa 1960.** Major upgrade to pulverised fuel systems. Main plant output increased to 74,500 kW; auxiliary system burning natural gas captured from coal mining added at around 49,500 kW. At the time, reportedly the largest privately owned power generation plant in Japan. [S1][S2]
- **16 October 1981.** Methane gas rupture causes an underground explosion and fire in Hokutan's Yubari New Mine (北炭夕張新炭鉱), killing 93 miners. (Date from Wikipedia coverage of the Hokutan Yubari incident; confirm in R2.) [S1][S2]
- **1991.** Power plant closes following the collapse of the Yubari coal industry and Hokutan's bankruptcy. [S1][S2]
- **2011.** Shimizusawa Project halts planned demolition and begins reopening parts of the site for tours and art. [S2]

## Architecture and site

- Coal-fired thermal power station with an associated hydroelectric dam upstream. [S1][S2]
- Sits within the broader Shimizusawa industrial complex, which includes the Old Hokutan Shimizusawa Ore Office and the Old Kitasumi Coal Analytical Laboratory remains. [S3]
- Built and expanded in phases: original 1926 plant, 1940 dam addition, circa 1960 pulverised-fuel upgrade and gas-burning auxiliary system. [S1][S2]

**Wording to use.** "Old Hokutan Shimizusawa Thermal Power Plant" is the form used by Japanese heritage and tourism sources. "Hokutan" is the common short form of Hokkaido Colliery and Steamship Co. (北海道炭礦汽船), not a brand name. In copy, either "Shimizusawa Thermal Power Plant" or "the old Hokutan Shimizusawa power plant" is fine; avoid calling it a "factory" or a "plant" without the word thermal/power.

## Machinery and operations

- **Fuel.** Coal-fired throughout. Post-1960, pulverised fuel systems. Auxiliary system burned natural gas captured from the coal mining process. [S1][S2]
- **Output timeline.**
  - 1926: 6,000 kW. [S1][S2]
  - 1940: plus 2,000 kW from the dam's hydroelectric plant. [S1][S2]
  - Circa 1960: 74,500 kW main plant, plus 49,500 kW auxiliary gas-burning system. [S1][S2]
- **Cooling water.** Supplied from Shimizusawa Dam, built for that purpose in 1940. [S1][S2]
- Detailed machinery inventory (boiler manufacturers, turbine makers, generator specifications) not yet captured. See Claims to verify.

## Role in wider context

- **Part of the Shimizusawa / Yubari coal complex.** Hokutan operated a network of mines across Yubari; the power plant was the generating heart of that network. [S1][S2]
- **Yubari's rise and collapse.** Yubari was a major Hokkaido coal city. Population peaked in the 1960s (over 100,000). Successive mine closures and the Yubari New Mine disaster in 1981 accelerated Hokutan's collapse; the 1991 closure of the power plant is part of that arc. [S1][S2]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back
to at least one verified fact.

1. **The generating heart of Yubari's coal industry.** 1926 to 1991, 65 years of coal-fired operation. [S1][S2]
2. **Largest privately owned power plant in Japan at its peak.** Circa 1960. Confirm scope of "largest" in R2. [S1][S2]
3. **Gas from the mines fed the turbines.** The 49,500 kW auxiliary system burned natural gas captured from coal mining. [S1][S2]
4. **A dam built to cool the plant.** The 1940 Shimizusawa Dam was purpose-built for cooling water and also generated hydroelectricity. [S1][S2]
5. **Saved from demolition.** The Shimizusawa Project halted planned demolition in 2011 and reopened parts of the site. [S2]
6. **The Yubari arc.** The plant closed in 1991 as Hokutan went bankrupt, part of a wider collapse that saw Yubari's population fall from 100,000 to under 10,000.

**Angles to avoid.** Avoid framing the 1981 Yubari New Mine disaster as colour; 93 miners died. Reference it if at all with restraint.
