## Chronology

- **1937.** NSW Railways Department electrification concept identifies the need for a dedicated generating station. [S3]
- **1941 (April).** W.H. Myers, Railways Chief Electrical Engineer (appointed 1925, retired 1946), submits formal proposal to the Electricity Advisory Committee. [S3]
- **1947.** Awaba State Mine opens; a 10.4 kilometre branch line is built to deliver coal to Wangi. [S3]
- **1948.** Civil works begin. First pair of 50 MW turbo-alternator sets ordered on 17 September. First four boilers ordered on 7 October. [S3]
- **1949.** Building drawings under way. Major-General Fewtrell, Railways Chief Civil Engineer, signs drawings into this year. [S3]
- **1950.** Electricity Commission Act passed; ownership pathway to Elcom established. [S3]
- **1951 (7 April).** First chimney construction begins; contractor Arcos Constructions Pty Ltd. [S3]
- **1952 (August).** First steelwork erected: 8,359 tonnes supplied by Sir William Arrol & Co., Glasgow. [S3]
- **1953 (1 January).** Ownership transferred from Railways to Electricity Commission of NSW under the Electricity Commission Act 1950. Railways remains the constructing authority. Site has 500 Railways Department employees plus approximately 100 contractor men. [S3]
- **1957 (6 November, 10:58 am).** Final A Station unit (boiler 3A) synchronised to the grid. [S3]
- **1958 (1 June, 12:30 pm).** No. 4 unit (60 MW) enters service, the largest single generating set in Australia at that time. [S3]
- **1958 (7 November).** Station officially opened by NSW Premier Joseph Cahill as the "Lake Macquarie (Wangi) Power Station". [S3]
- **1959.** Renamed Wangi Power Station. [S3]
- **1959 (April).** No. 4 boiler enters service. [S3]
- **1959 (July).** No. 4 boiler furnace explosion; economiser distortion, repaired August 1959. [S3]
- **1959 (2 November, 2:15 pm).** No. 5 unit enters service. [S3]
- **1960 (24 August).** No. 6 unit enters service. All six sets now operational. Second cooling-water tunnel commissioned the same year. [S3]
- **1964 (10 June).** Wangi plays a major role in restoring power to NSW after the total state power shutdown. Restoration achieved via the stoker-fired boilers of A Station, whose fires remained alight through the shutdown. [S3]
- **1964.** Peak operating year: 2,439.713 GWh generated, 1.2 million tonnes of coal consumed, approximately 400 employees. [S3]
- **1973 (23 February).** Last steam-hauled coal train to Wangi (loco 6042). Road haulage takes over from late 1973. [S3]
- **1975 to 1976.** Ducon Micropul fabric filter trial on site, as a precursor to Eraring. [S3]
- **1985 (7 March).** A Station retired. [S3]
- **1986 (31 October).** B Station closes. [S3]
- **1989.** Formal decommissioning. [S3]
- **1995 to 1997.** Generating equipment removed from site. [S3]
- **1998 (18 March).** Pacific Power s.170 register No. 79 gazetted. [S3]
- **1998.** Main power station site sold by Pacific Power via public tender to I.J. McDonald Pty Ltd (Brisbane), acquired by Ian McDonald for a proposed apartment / motel / cinema redevelopment. [S3]
- **1999 (2 April).** NSW State Heritage Register Listing 01014 gazetted. [S3]
- **2002.** Centennial Coal (Centennial Fassifern Pty Ltd) takes over Myuna Colliery. [S3]
- **2017.** Fire at the site. [S3]
- **2018 (May).** I.J. McDonald Pty Ltd in winding-up process following Ian McDonald's death; redevelopment stalled. [S3]
- **2020 onwards.** University of Newcastle Hunter Living Histories project, led by Prof. Michael Chapman (School of Architecture and Built Environment) with Gionni Di Gravio OAM (UoN Cultural Collections), undertakes point-cloud and drone scanning and a 3D documentation model. The UoN Cultural Collections hold more than 8,000 archival drawings of the station. [S2][S3]

## Architecture and site

- Sited on the western shore of Lake Macquarie at Wangi Wangi. Cooling water intake at Myuna Bay, discharge at Wangi Bay. [S3]
- **Main building: 12,000 square metres, 11 storeys, 228 metres long by 53.3 metres wide by 41 metres high.** Total complex length 291 metres. Triple-brick cladding over a riveted steel frame on a concrete base. [S3]
- **Three reinforced concrete chimneys.** 16-sided, 76 metres high, 6 metres internal diameter. [S3]
- **Cooling water tunnels.** Tunnel 1 is a 577-metre horseshoe section (3.65 by 4.1 metres, 454,600 litres per minute). Tunnel 2, commissioned 1960, is 3.95 by 3.95 metres with a 1,360,800-litre-per-minute capacity. Peak combined cooling-water use 19.3 cubic metres per second. [S3]
- **Water storage.** Three 2.27-million-litre tanks on the hill behind the station. [S3]
- **Project architect: Colin Smith of C.H. Smith & Johnson, Architects.** Smith was also architect for several other early Electricity Commission of NSW projects. The original Railways Department architect for the station has not been identified. [S3]
- Architectural appearance treated as an integral design parameter, not subordinate to engineering or economics. A rare example in Australia of industrial architecture approached that way. [S3]
- Landscape and environment considered at the forefront of design, not just efficiency. [S3]

**Wording to use.** "Wangi Power Station" is the standard form on the heritage listing. "Wangi Wangi Power Station" is also correct and appears in some local and Wikipedia contexts. The correct project architect is Colin Smith of C.H. Smith & Johnson; do not attribute the building to Cobden Parkes (this is a common secondary-source error). The station has two distinct parts: A Station (the 1957 to 1958 stoker-fired section, 3 × 50 MW) and B Station (the 1958 to 1960 pulverised-coal section, 3 × 60 MW).

## Machinery and operations

- Coal-fired steam generation. Two stations under one roof: A Station (stoker-fired, 150 MW) and B Station (pulverised-coal, 180 MW). Total 330 MW. [S3]
- **Boilers.** A Station: 6 × Babcock & Wilcox "High Head" stoker-fired boilers. B Station: 3 × Babcock & Wilcox "Radiant" single-drum water-tube pulverised-coal boilers. [S3]
- **Turbines.** Six Parsons three-cylinder tandem compound steam turbines (three at 50 MW, three at 60 MW). All supplied by C.A. Parsons & Co. [S3]
- **Australian firsts.** B Station hosted the first hydrogen-cooled alternators in any Australian power station. B Station was also the first Australian power station to burn pulverised coal. [S3]
- **Test-bed role.** Wangi hosted BHP/BP coal slurry and agglomeration trials; the first full-scale test of UNSW plasma-arc ignition of pulverised-fuel boilers; and the 1975 to 1976 Ducon Micropul fabric-filter trial that was the precursor to the filters installed at Eraring. [S3]
- **Training.** Wangi was described in the heritage listing as "one of the most advanced training centres for power station operations in the southern hemisphere". [S3]
- **Coal supply.** Awaba State Mine (from 1947, via a 10.4 km branch line) and Newstan Colliery (first deliveries 1957, under Joint Coal Board from 1951). Last steam-hauled coal train 23 February 1973. [S3]
- **Lifetime output.** 36,181.16 GWh over 28 years of service. 20 million tonnes of coal consumed. Peak year 1964: 2,439.713 GWh, 1.2 million tonnes of coal, approximately 400 employees. [S3]
- **Equipment removal.** The bulk of the generating equipment was removed between 1995 and 1997. What survives in the shell is the architectural and structural fabric, not the working machinery. [S3]

## Heritage status

- **NSW State Heritage Register Listing 01014**, gazetted 2 April 1999 in Government Gazette 82-2018 p. 5542. Heritage NSW DB No. 5014146. File references S97/00082/001 and EF14/4831. Curtilage plan SHR Plan No. 2721. [S3]
- **Pacific Power s.170 register entry No. 79**, gazetted 18 March 1998. [S3]
- Listing language (paraphrased from S3):
  - Highest-level State significance for its association with the evolution of coalfields-sited power stations and power generation in NSW.
  - Played a pre-eminent part in relieving NSW from the drastic power shortages and blackouts of the late 1950s.
  - Played a major role in restoring power to NSW after the total state power shutdown of 10 June 1964.
  - Rare example in Australia of a modern power station where architectural appearance and landscape setting were treated as integral to the design rather than secondary to engineering.

## Role in wider context

- One of a series of coalfields-sited power stations built in NSW in the postwar period to address the state's electricity shortfall. [S3]
- **Once the largest power station in NSW**, and holder of that title for the first years of its operation. [S1][S3]
- **Grid-transfer milestone.** Built by NSW Railways, operated by the Electricity Commission of NSW from 1 January 1953 under the Electricity Commission Act 1950. The transition is a node point in the institutional history of electricity in NSW. [S3]
- **Technology-first milestone.** B Station introduced the first hydrogen-cooled alternators in Australia and was the first Australian power station to burn pulverised coal. [S3]
- **Workforce profile.** Around 500 Railways Department staff plus 100 contractor men during construction, falling to approximately 400 employees at the 1964 operating peak. On-site housing included single and married quarters and a construction-era tent city. Significant post-war Italian migrant labour. [S3]
- **Industrial relations.** Wangi figured in NSW power-industry industrial action over the 35-hour working week in the late 1960s. [S3]
- **Archival footprint.** More than 8,000 engineering and architectural drawings are held in the University of Newcastle Cultural Collections, with the Hunter Living Histories project providing a public-facing research portal. [S2][S3]

## People

- **W.H. Myers.** NSW Railways Chief Electrical Engineer. Appointed 1925, retired 1946. Submitted the formal proposal for the Wangi station to the Electricity Advisory Committee in April 1941. [S3]
- **Major-General Fewtrell.** NSW Railways Chief Civil Engineer. Signed off construction drawings as late as 1949. [S3]
- **Colin Smith of C.H. Smith & Johnson, Architects.** Project architect for Elcom. Also architect for several other early Electricity Commission of NSW projects. The "eminent Architect" referred to in the SHR heritage listing. [S3]
- **Joseph Cahill, NSW Premier.** Officially opened the station on 7 November 1958 as the "Lake Macquarie (Wangi) Power Station". [S1][S3]
- **Sir William Arrol & Co., Glasgow.** Steelwork contractor. Supplied 8,359 tonnes of steel; first steelwork erected August 1952. [S3]
- **Arcos Constructions Pty Ltd.** Built the first chimney; construction start 7 April 1951. [S3]
- **Other civil contractors.** Dorman Long; The Electric Power Transmission Co.; F. Compton and Sons; Norman King and Partners; James Wallace & Co. [S3]
- **Ian McDonald / I.J. McDonald Pty Ltd.** Brisbane-based owner of the main power station site from 1998. Proposed an apartment, motel and cinema redevelopment. Redevelopment stalled after his death. His company was in winding-up process as of May 2018. [S3]
- **Prof. Michael Chapman.** University of Newcastle, School of Architecture and Built Environment. Lead investigator on the Hunter Living Histories Wangi Power Station Project from around 2020. [S2]
- **Gionni Di Gravio OAM.** University Archivist, UoN Cultural Collections. Co-lead on the Wangi drawings archive and the Hunter Living Histories project. [S2]

## Redevelopment and current use

- Main power station site (Lot 101 DP 880089) sold by Pacific Power in 1998 via public tender to I.J. McDonald Pty Ltd, Brisbane. Ian McDonald proposed an apartment, motel and cinema redevelopment. The project stalled after his death; the owning company was in a winding-up process as of May 2018. [S3]
- Myuna Colliery, immediately west of the main building, remains operational under Centennial Fassifern Pty Ltd (Centennial Coal, from 2002). It occupies approximately 15.5 hectares of the complex. [S3]
- Other lots within the original curtilage are held by NSW National Parks and Wildlife Service and Crown Lands. [S3]
- Equipment removal 1995 to 1997. What remains in the shell is architectural and structural fabric, not working plant. [S3]
- 2017 fire on site. [S3]
- From around 2020 the University of Newcastle, via the Hunter Living Histories program, has been producing high-resolution point-cloud and drone surveys of the complex, alongside a 3D documentation model, drawing on the >8,000 archival drawings held in UoN Cultural Collections. [S2][S3]

## Narrative themes and angles

Use these as scaffolding for copy across formats. Each angle ties back to at least one verified fact.

1. **The largest in the state, for a time.** Once the largest power station in NSW. A Station plus B Station gave 330 MW under one 228-metre roof. [S3]
2. **Architecture as intent, not afterthought.** One of the few Australian power stations of its era designed with architectural appearance and landscape setting treated as integral parameters, not consequences of the engineering. The project architect was Colin Smith of C.H. Smith & Johnson. [S3]
3. **Two stations under one roof.** A Station (1957 to 1958, stoker-fired, 3 × 50 MW) and B Station (1958 to 1960, pulverised-coal, 3 × 60 MW). Australia's first pulverised-coal station. Australia's first hydrogen-cooled alternators. The building holds the shift from one generation of coal-fired technology to the next. [S3]
4. **The 1964 blackout.** On 10 June 1964 the NSW grid went dark. Wangi's stoker-fired A Station boilers had kept their fires alight and the station played a major role in bringing the state back. [S3]
5. **A test bed, not just a power station.** Australia's first pulverised-coal boiler; first hydrogen-cooled alternators; BHP/BP coal slurry and agglomeration trials; the first full-scale test of UNSW plasma-arc ignition; the Ducon Micropul fabric-filter trial of 1975 to 1976 that precede the filters at Eraring. [S3]
6. **Ten years up, thirty years running, forty years down.** Civil works from 1948, first synchronisation 1957, formal opening 1958, decommissioned 1989, and still standing. [S3]
7. **Built by Railways, run by Elcom.** Proposed in 1941 by the Railways Chief Electrical Engineer; construction began under Railways; operation transferred to the Electricity Commission of NSW on 1 January 1953. Wangi is a node point in the institutional history of NSW electricity. [S3]
8. **An archival shadow.** More than 8,000 engineering and architectural drawings of the station are held at the University of Newcastle. The physical building is the visible remainder; the paper record is as large as the structure. [S2][S3]
9. **The unbuilt second life.** Bought by I.J. McDonald Pty Ltd in 1998 for an apartment, motel and cinema conversion that never happened. The building has been waiting for a redevelopment for nearly as long as it generated power. [S3]

**Angles to avoid.** Romanticising the building as "frozen in time" — the generating equipment was removed between 1995 and 1997. What remains is the architectural shell and the landscape relationship, not a full machine in situ. Do not attribute the architecture to Cobden Parkes; the architect of record per the SHR is Colin Smith. Do not describe the station as a single plant; always note the A Station / B Station split when technical detail is relevant.

## Things worth noting in photographs

- **The three 76-metre reinforced-concrete chimneys.** 16-sided, 6 metres internal diameter. The tallest elements of the complex. [S3]
- **The main building.** 228 metres long, 53.3 metres wide, 41 metres high; 11 storeys. Triple-brick cladding over a riveted steel frame on a concrete base. A particularly long continuous industrial facade by Australian standards. [S3]
- **Lakeside siting** on the western shore of Lake Macquarie, with built form set against water and landscape — the architectural intent the heritage listing specifically calls out. [S3]
- **The empty turbine hall.** Generating plant was removed 1995 to 1997; the surviving volume is the most architecturally legible part of the interior. [S3]
- **The cooling-water intake at Myuna Bay and discharge at Wangi Bay.** The tunnel system is buried but its surface structures are identifiable. [S3]
- **A / B Station split.** The three 50 MW stoker-fired units of A Station and the three 60 MW pulverised-coal units of B Station occupied different bays of the building. [S3]
- **Three water storage tanks** on the hill behind the station; 2.27 million litres each. [S3]
- **2017 fire damage** may be visible depending on where photographs were taken. [S3]
